Worked Example: Fixed Effects vs Random Effects

A side-by-side numerical walkthrough. Tiny dataset, real OLS/LMM fits, every coefficient unpacked. Linear outcome (not logit) so closed-form answers exist — the logic carries directly to logit, only the estimator changes (Laplace approximation instead of OLS).

The one-sentence summary: Both models put the same agent-level offset on the right side of the equation. FE calls it $\alpha_k$ and lets it be anything. RE calls it $\gamma X_k + u_k$ and forces $u_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_u^2)$ with $\mathrm{cov}(u_k, X_k) = 0$. Those two assumptions are what buy you the trait coefficient $\gamma$ — and the price is shrinkage on $u_k$.

§1 The setup

Five agents, one binary trait extraverted, six observations each at varying infection levels. The true data-generating process:

$$ y_{ik} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \mathrm{infection}_{ik} + \gamma \cdot \mathrm{extraverted}_k + u_k + \varepsilon_{ik} $$

with true values $\beta_0 = 0$, $\beta_1 = 0.3$, $\gamma = -1.0$, $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.4^2)$.

Notice: introvert mean $u$ = +0.4; extravert mean $u$ = −0.27. Trait and unobserved baseline are slightly correlated even in this small toy world (we'll see why this matters in §5).

§2 Both models, side by side

Fixed Effects

$$ y_{ik} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \mathrm{inf}_{ik} + \sum_{k=B,C,D,E} \alpha_k D_{ik} + \varepsilon_{ik} $$
  • 4 dummies (one per agent, A is reference)
  • $\alpha_k$ is a free parameter per agent
  • $\gamma$ cannot be estimated — trait is perfectly collinear with the dummies
  • Estimator: OLS

Random Effects

$$ y_{ik} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \mathrm{inf}_{ik} + \gamma X_k + u_k + \varepsilon_{ik} $$ $$ u_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_u^2), \quad \mathrm{cov}(u_k, X_k) = 0 $$
  • Trait $X_k$ explicitly in the model, no dummies
  • $u_k$ is a random draw from a normal
  • $\gamma$ is estimable — the distributional assumption breaks the collinearity
  • Estimator: maximum likelihood (LMM)

Two things to notice:
  1. $\hat\gamma = -1.56$ overshoots the truth $-1.00$. Why? Trait is mildly correlated with the true $u_k$ in this small sample (introverts happen to have higher $u$). RE absorbs that correlation into $\gamma$ because the model assumes $\mathrm{cov}(u, X) = 0$.

§6 So were you off base?

Your hunch was: "functionally the traits exist in the random effect and we have to assume normality for agent baselines."

That's basically right. What this worked example shows concretely:
  • FE has 5 agent baselines as free parameters. RE has the same 5 baselines decomposed into $\gamma X_k + u_k$, with $u_k$ normal.
  • The normality assumption is what lets you separate "trait part" from "residual personality part" — without it, you'd be back to FE's "one number per agent, can't say why."
  • The second hidden assumption — $\mathrm{cov}(u, X) = 0$ — is what makes $\hat\gamma$ a clean trait coefficient. Violating it (as we did slightly here) biases $\hat\gamma$.
  • You don't get this for free: the price is shrinkage. BLUPs are pulled toward the trait-implied mean, especially when within-agent observations are noisy or few.

§7 Diagnostics: checking the two RE assumptions

You named them correctly. RE buys you the trait coefficient $\gamma$ in exchange for two assumptions:

  1. Normality: $u_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_u^2)$
  2. Exogeneity: $\mathrm{cov}(u_k, X_k) = 0$

These are not equally testable. Here's what you can actually check.

§7.1 BLUP normality (testable, standard)

Three things to do, in order of effort:

  • Q-Q plot of estimated BLUPs $\hat u_k$ vs. normal quantiles. Standard output from lme4::ranef(). Look for systematic curvature, especially heavy tails.
  • Shapiro–Wilk test on the BLUPs. With $n = 99$ in Paper 3 it has reasonable power.
  • DHARMa scaled-residual diagnostics: tests calibration, dispersion, outliers all at once. You already use this in Appendix C.
Important caveat about BLUP Q-Q plots: BLUPs are shrunken estimates of the $u_k$, not the true values. Their variance is intentionally smaller than $\sigma_u^2$. So the Q-Q plot tests shape, not scale — it's fine for spotting skewness or heavy tails but don't read variance off it.

In our toy data with only 5 agents, the Q-Q plot is uninformative (BLUPs: +0.68, −0.68, +0.70, −0.49, −0.21 → roughly symmetric, but $n = 5$ is too few to say anything). In Paper 3 with $n = 99$, this is a real check.

§7.2 Exogeneity $\mathrm{cov}(u_k, X_k) = 0$ (partly testable, mostly an assumption)

Look at what happened in our toy data. The true $u_k$ and trait $X_k$:

Sample correlation: $r(u, X) = -0.44$. Introverts ($X=0$) happen to have higher $u$ than extraverts ($X=1$). This violates the RE exogeneity assumption.

Consequence: RE absorbs that correlation into $\hat\gamma$. True $\gamma = -1.00$; estimated $\hat\gamma = -1.56$. The trait coefficient is biased more-negative because the unobserved baseline is correlated with the trait. This is the price of getting $\gamma$ at all — and it's why you check.

§7.3 The Hausman test — for the parts you CAN test

Hausman's idea: FE is unbiased even when $\mathrm{cov}(u, X) \neq 0$ (it doesn't need that assumption). RE is more efficient but biased if exogeneity fails. So if FE and RE give materially different estimates of the common coefficients, exogeneity is violated.

$$ H = (\hat\beta_{FE} - \hat\beta_{RE})^\top \left[\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(\hat\beta_{FE}) - \widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(\hat\beta_{RE})\right]^{-1} (\hat\beta_{FE} - \hat\beta_{RE}) $$ Under $H_0$ (exogeneity holds): $H \sim \chi^2_k$, where $k$ is the dimension of $\beta_{\text{common}}$.

In our toy: $\hat\beta_{1,\text{FE}} = \hat\beta_{1,\text{RE}} = 0.2761$. Hausman would not reject — but Hausman only tests common coefficients (the infection slope), not $\gamma$ itself.

§7.4 The Mundlak / correlated-random-effects trick

Add the within-agent mean $\bar X_k$ of each time-varying predictor as an extra regressor:

$$ y_{ik} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_{ik} + \lambda \bar X_k + u_k + \varepsilon_{ik} $$

If $\lambda$ is significant, $X$ is correlated with $u$. This is essentially equivalent to Hausman and easier to fit.

The catch that hits Paper 3 directly: Big-Five traits are time-invariant — they don't change across infection levels. So $\bar X_k = X_k$ for every agent. The Mundlak regressor is collinear with the trait itself, and you cannot separately identify "trait effect" from "covariance with $u$." This is a fundamental identification problem for RE with time-invariant predictors, not a quirk of Paper 3.

§7.5 What you can actually defend in Paper 3

  • ✅ BLUP normality: show the Q-Q plot, report DHARMa diagnostics. Standard, fully testable.
  • ✅ Hausman on the within-curve coefficients (e.g., infection-level slope appears in both Reg 2 FE and Reg 3 RE). If they agree, that's evidence the model is well-specified on its shared dimensions.
  • ⚠️ Trait–baseline exogeneity: not separately testable for time-invariant traits. The defense is by design: Big-Five traits were assigned to agents independently of any LLM behavior, so by construction there is no selection mechanism that would induce $\mathrm{cov}(u, X) \neq 0$.
  • ⚠️ Acknowledge as a limitation: "the random-intercept exogeneity assumption is not separately testable for time-invariant person-level predictors; our defense rests on the experimental assignment of agent traits."
How to phrase it to the committee:
"We check normality of the random intercepts via Q-Q plots and DHARMa diagnostics, which look good. We check Hausman-style agreement on the within-curve coefficients shared between Reg 2 and Reg 3. The exogeneity of agent traits relative to the random intercept is not separately testable because traits are time-invariant — but we defend it on design grounds: traits were assigned independently of the LLM, so there is no selection mechanism that would correlate them with unobserved agent-level baselines."
