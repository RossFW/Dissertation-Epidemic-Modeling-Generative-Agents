Comparing Two Mobility Curves

Polynomial regression (Williams 2023 style) vs exponential decay with embedded comparisons. Same simulated dataset, both methods applied, then a verdict.

The headline you'll read at the end: When you're just asking "are these two curves different?" the polynomial answers correctly. When you ask "how are they different?" the polynomial can mislead — and the exponential gives a mechanistically clean answer (intercept shift vs floor shift vs decay-rate shift).

§1 The two-curve question

You have mobility curves from two LLM configurations. Each curve is 40 averaged binary-decision points across infection levels 0–7%. You want to answer:

  1. Are these two curves statistically different?
  2. If yes, how — different level? different sensitivity to infection? different floor?

The choice of functional form determines what kind of "different" you can detect cleanly.

§2 The polynomial method (Williams 2023 style)

You stack both curves' data into one dataset, add a dummy $D \in \{0, 1\}$ (0 = baseline, 1 = treatment), and fit two nested OLS models:

Baseline-shift model: $$ m_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \mathrm{inf}_i + \beta_2 \mathrm{inf}_i^2 + \delta D_i + \varepsilon_i $$ Test: $H_0: \delta = 0$. A significant $\delta$ means a vertical shift between the curves.
Baseline-plus-slope-shift model: $$ m_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \mathrm{inf}_i + \beta_2 \mathrm{inf}_i^2 + \delta D_i + \delta_{\text{slope}} D_i \mathrm{inf}_i + \varepsilon_i $$ Tests: $H_0: \delta = 0$ (level shift) and $H_0: \delta_{\text{slope}} = 0$ (linear-slope shift).

Two t-tests on two coefficients. That's it. Reviewers can read it from a regression table.

§3 The exponential method

If you believe mobility decays exponentially from an unperturbed value $a$ toward a floor $c$ at rate $b$:

$$ m_i = c + (a - c) e^{-b \cdot \mathrm{inf}_i} + \varepsilon_i $$

Each parameter has a mechanistic interpretation:

  • $a$ — unperturbed mobility at infection = 0
  • $c$ — floor mobility at infection → ∞ (people who can't or won't stay home)
  • $b$ — decay rate (sensitivity of behavior to infection signal)

There are three reasonable ways to test for differences between curves:

(a) Fit separately, contrast each parameter. Fit $(a_A, b_A, c_A)$ and $(a_B, b_B, c_B)$ independently via nonlinear least squares. Use the SE from each fit and a z-test: $$ z = \frac{\hat\theta_B - \hat\theta_A}{\sqrt{\mathrm{SE}_A^2 + \mathrm{SE}_B^2}} $$ Gives you three tests — one for each parameter.
(b) Embed dummies, joint fit. Parameterize as $a = a_0 + \delta_a D$, etc., fit all 6 parameters jointly. Wald tests on $\delta_a, \delta_b, \delta_c$. Equivalent to (a) with slightly more efficiency.
(c) Nested F-test. Fit a restricted model (one set of $(a,b,c)$ for all data) and a full model (separate $(a,b,c)$ per curve). Compare SSEs: $$ F = \frac{(\mathrm{SSE}_R - \mathrm{SSE}_F)/(p_F - p_R)}{\mathrm{SSE}_F/(n - p_F)} $$ Single test: "are these curves the same exponential?" Doesn't tell you how they differ — just whether.

Where did that SE come from?

The separate-fits contrast SE uses the standard rule for the variance of a difference of independent estimators:

$$ \mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta_B - \hat\theta_A) = \mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta_A) + \mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta_B) - 2\,\mathrm{Cov}(\hat\theta_A, \hat\theta_B) $$

Curves A and B come from independent runs (different LLM configs, different random seeds), so $\mathrm{Cov}(\hat\theta_A, \hat\theta_B) = 0$ and the formula collapses to:

$$ \mathrm{SE}(\hat\theta_B - \hat\theta_A) = \sqrt{\mathrm{SE}_A^2 + \mathrm{SE}_B^2} $$

For the decay rate $b$: $\sqrt{0.0246^2 + 0.0369^2} = \sqrt{0.001967} = 0.0443$ ✓ — that's the SE in the contrast table above.

The individual $\mathrm{SE}_A$ and $\mathrm{SE}_B$ come from the asymptotic covariance matrix of each NLS fit:

$$ \widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(\hat\theta) = \hat\sigma^2 \left(J^\top J\right)^{-1} $$

where $J$ is the Jacobian of the regression function with respect to $\theta = (a, b, c)$ evaluated at the estimates, and $\hat\sigma^2$ is the residual variance. Same logic as OLS, just with a nonlinear $J$.

(b) Embedded-dummy exponential — the direct analog of the polynomial $\delta$

This is the version that mirrors what you're already doing in the polynomial regression. One joint fit, one dummy, one Wald test per parameter shift:

$$ m_i \;=\; c(D_i) \;+\; \bigl(a(D_i) - c(D_i)\bigr)\, \exp\!\left(-b(D_i)\cdot \mathrm{inf}_i\right) \;+\; \varepsilon_i $$ where $$ a(D_i) = a_0 + \delta_a D_i, \quad b(D_i) = b_0 + \delta_b D_i, \quad c(D_i) = c_0 + \delta_c D_i $$

$D_i = 0$ for baseline observations, $D_i = 1$ for treatment. Six parameters fit jointly via NLS; the covariance matrix is $\hat\sigma^2(J^\top J)^{-1}$ with $J$ now $80 \times 6$. Each $\delta$ has its own SE pulled directly from the diagonal — exactly like a polynomial regression dummy.

§6.5 Quick reference card — the two questions answered

Question 1: Where did the separate-fits contrast SE come from?

The standard variance-of-a-difference rule for two independent estimators:

$$ \mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta_B - \hat\theta_A) = \mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta_A) + \mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta_B) - 2\,\mathrm{Cov}(\hat\theta_A, \hat\theta_B) $$

Curves A and B come from independent runs, so $\mathrm{Cov}(\hat\theta_A, \hat\theta_B) = 0$, and:

$$ \mathrm{SE}(\hat\theta_B - \hat\theta_A) = \sqrt{\mathrm{SE}_A^2 + \mathrm{SE}_B^2} $$

Worked numerically for the decay rate $b$:

$$ \sqrt{0.0246^2 + 0.0369^2} = \sqrt{0.001967} = 0.0443 $$

Each individual $\mathrm{SE}_A, \mathrm{SE}_B$ comes from the NLS asymptotic covariance matrix of its own fit:

$$ \widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(\hat\theta) = \hat\sigma^2 \left(J^\top J\right)^{-1} $$

where $J$ is the Jacobian of the regression function with respect to $(a, b, c)$ at the estimates.

Question 2: What is the dummy-variable equivalent — the direct analog of the polynomial $\delta$?

One joint NLS fit with the dummy embedded in every parameter:

$$ m_i \;=\; c(D_i) \;+\; \bigl(a(D_i) - c(D_i)\bigr)\, \exp\!\left(-b(D_i)\cdot \mathrm{inf}_i\right) \;+\; \varepsilon_i $$ with $$ a(D_i) = a_0 + \delta_a D_i, \quad b(D_i) = b_0 + \delta_b D_i, \quad c(D_i) = c_0 + \delta_c D_i $$

$D_i = 0$ for baseline observations, $D_i = 1$ for treatment. Six parameters fit jointly; three Wald tests answer:

  • $\delta_a$ : is the initial level different?
  • $\delta_b$ : is the decay rate different?
  • $\delta_c$ : is the floor different?

Results on the simulated data: $\delta_a = -0.0055\ (p = 0.75,\ \text{NS})$, $\delta_b = +0.31\ (p = 2 \times 10^{-12},\ \text{sig})$, $\delta_c = -0.0056\ (p = 0.76,\ \text{NS})$. Same conclusion as the separate-fits contrasts; slightly tighter SEs because the joint fit pools residual variance across 80 observations.

Parallel to the polynomial regression:
  • Polynomial: $\delta$ tests level shift, $\delta_{\text{slope}}$ tests slope shift. One joint fit, Wald tests.
  • Exponential-dummy: $\delta_a$ tests initial-level shift, $\delta_b$ tests decay-rate shift, $\delta_c$ tests floor shift. One joint fit, Wald tests.
Only differences: three parameters vs two, and NLS instead of OLS. The hypothesis-testing machinery is identical.

§7 So is polynomial defensible for two-curve comparison?

Yes — with a caveat.
  • Polynomial reliably detects whether two curves differ. The $\delta$ coefficient is a sensitive omnibus indicator: any consistent gap between the two curves shows up as a significant level shift.
  • What polynomial can't tell you cleanly is how they differ. A faster decay rate, a lower floor, a lower initial level — all show up as "level shift" in $\delta$. The $\delta_{\text{slope}}$ coefficient only catches differences in the linear component of the curve, and even then mixes with shape changes.
  • For Paper 3's stated goal — "is configuration X different from baseline" — the polynomial is fully defensible, and it matches Williams 2023. The mechanistic question of why they differ lives in Reg 1–4 (the binary-decision logit regressions), not in the curve-comparison stage.

§8 When you'd want to switch to exponential

  • You want to report differences in sensitivity to infection per se — i.e., the decay rate is the scientific quantity of interest.
  • You want to report a floor mobility — the asymptotic minimum that essential-worker-type agents represent.
  • You want the model to respect the [0, 1] bound for mobility (quadratic doesn't).
  • You're comparing curves at a wider range of infection levels where quadratic stops being a good local approximation.

§9 Recommendation for the committee meeting

If Joe (or anyone) asks why polynomial:

"The polynomial comparison is doing descriptive curve-shape testing in the framework Williams 2023 established — it's a sensitive omnibus detector of differences between two configurations. The mechanistic 'why' isn't carried by these regressions; it's carried by the logit regressions on individual binary decisions, which properly model mobility as a bounded proportion. An exponential decay form would give a more mechanistic decomposition of 'initial level vs decay rate vs floor,' and we'd consider it for the next paper, but the current polynomial does its job for this paper's question."

If asked the deeper question of whether the polynomial has misled you on any specific result in the paper: that's worth checking — re-fit one or two of the most consequential pairwise comparisons with the exponential and see whether the level-vs-decay-rate story changes. If they tell the same story, the polynomial is robust enough to keep.
