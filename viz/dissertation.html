Abstract

Generative agent-based modeling (GABM) replaces the rule-based decision-making of classical agent-based models with agent decisions powered by large language models (LLM), enabling researchers to simulate human behavior that resists clean mathematical specification. This dissertation establishes the methodology, characterizes its sensitivity to prompt design, and characterizes its sensitivity to the underlying LLM. Three studies — conducted within a shared epidemic GABM framework in which generative agents decide each day whether to stay home or go out — support the central claim: GABM is a viable simulation method, but the prompt and the LLM are both parameters of any GABM finding.

Agents endogenously self-isolate as community case counts rise and quarantine when they feel ill. Collectively, this produces multi-wave dynamics followed by an endemic period. Heterogeneous behavior emerges from Big Five personality traits, age, and gender.

The prompt is a parameter of GABM, but only along certain dimensions. Synonymous rewording and persona names leave epidemic trajectories statistically unchanged; minor wording variations and contextual shifts both alter outcomes.

LLM choice is also a parameter. Across 21 LLM configurations from OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google, most LLMs agree on the direction of persona effects but disagree several-fold on magnitude. Standard model attributes — size, version, knowledge cutoff, release date — do not predict where an LLM’s collective agent behavior lands. Mentioning a trait in the response text moves that trait’s effect on the decision beyond the trait alone.

Together the three studies argue that GABM is most defensible for qualitative claims about agent behavior and weakest for the quantitative claims modelers most often want to make. LLM sensitivity testing should become standard practice in GABM publications, alongside prompt sensitivity. The methodology is real; its parameters include the prompt and the LLM.

General Audience Abstract

Artificial intelligence has reached the point where large language models — the technology behind systems like ChatGPT and Claude — can hold convincing conversations and reason about everyday situations. A natural question follows: can we use these models to simulate the behavior of large groups of people during real-world events like epidemics, helping researchers and policymakers test interventions before deploying them? This approach is called generative agent-based modeling. In one of these simulations, hundreds of artificial agents, each given a personality, background, and current situation, let a language model decide what they would do, day by day, as a scenario unfolds.

This dissertation tests the approach in a simulated epidemic, where each AI-driven agent decides each day whether to stay home or go out. It asks three questions: does the method work, does the wording of the AI’s instructions matter, and does the choice of AI model matter? The central finding: the approach works, and both the wording and the AI model materially shape every result.

Given basic information about their own symptoms and the daily case count in their town, the AI agents make sensible decisions: they stay home when they feel sick, and they isolate when cases rise. Collectively, the simulated population produces patterns that resemble real epidemics, including multiple infection waves followed by a low and consistent prevalence period. Individual agents behave differently depending on the personality traits, age, and gender they were assigned.

The wording of the instructions matters, but only in specific ways. Rewording the instructions using synonyms, or changing the names of the simulated people, produces no measurable difference in outcomes. Small but meaningful changes, like slightly different word choices or shifts in the situation being described, do change outcomes. Researchers can treat the first kind of variation as safe, but need to check the second.

The choice of AI model also matters. Across 21 different AI models from three major companies — OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google — the models agree on the general direction of how personality affects behavior, but disagree by several-fold on how strong those effects are. Standard attributes like size, version, training-data age, and release date fail to predict which AI model will produce which behavior. The agents’ own words also matter: mentioning a personality trait in their response shifts the trait’s effect on the decision.

Together, the three studies suggest this approach is most trustworthy for broad qualitative claims — “people respond to rising cases by staying home” — and least trustworthy for precise quantitative claims about the size of those effects. Future researchers should test both the AI model and the wording of its instructions before drawing conclusions. The method is real and useful; the AI model and the prompt are both variables that researchers must test and report.

Chapter 1: Introduction

1.1 Problem Context and Motivation

To date, COVID-19 has been linked to 7.1 million reported deaths worldwide who_covid_deaths. A toll of that scale is a reminder that the epidemic models guiding public-health policy, and the decisions they inform, carry real consequences for how many lives are saved lim2023_responsiveness. Those models are only as good as their assumptions about human behavior: the pandemic made plain how central social distancing, mask use, and vaccination are to shaping epidemic trajectories nature2020,kraemer2020. Risk perception and disease spread co-evolve: behavior reshapes transmission, new transmission levels reshape perceived risk, and the cycle iterates rahmandad2022_missing. Yet most epidemic forecasts treat behavior as fixed or inject exogenous time-series data xu2021. Only a few models have endogenously linked human behavior as a response to the state of the disease rahmandad2021_dynamics,rahmandad2022_forecasting; the absence of this link produces biases in long-term projections rahmandad2022_forecasting and suboptimal policy guidance rahmandad2021_vaccinating. Embedding human behavior in epidemic models is therefore essential to improve forecast accuracy and policy impact rahmandad2022_missing.

Several obstacles have hindered the systematic integration of behavior into epidemic models funk2015. Human behavior is heterogeneous, differing across individuals, cultures, and communities, making it difficult to mathematically represent or parameterize a person. The epidemiology field also lacks a unified theory of how behavior changes under infectious disease risk weston2020. Acquiring reliable behavioral data from an epidemic is challenging verelst2016, which causes many studies to omit behavioral feedback entirely. Recent advances in generative artificial intelligence offer a path forward: a way to emulate human behavior that can be coupled directly with disease transmission, improving the forecasts that drive public-health policy.

1.2 Theoretical Background: Generative Agent-Based Modeling

Generative artificial intelligence — particularly large language models (LLMs) — has demonstrated an unprecedented capacity to simulate context-aware decision making. When supplied with a textual “persona” and environmental cues, LLM-powered agents, known as generative agents, can reason and act in ways that bear qualitative resemblance to human behavior park2023,binz_schulz2023,akata2023. This dissertation introduces and probes the generative agent-based modeling (GABM) paradigm: an agent-based model in which each agent’s decision at each timestep is produced by a large language model rather than by hand-coded behavioral rules. At each step the LLM receives a natural-language prompt describing the agent’s persona and current situation, and its reply is parsed into a structured action that advances the simulation to the next step ghaffarzadegan2024. In this dissertation the paradigm is instantiated for an epidemic: each agent is prompted with its persona, health status, and the local case count, and decides whether to stay home or go out.

Williams et al. williams2023 introduced this epidemic GABM, demonstrating that generative agents can produce realistic behavior at both the individual and population levels (Chapter 2). As the method has spread, two recent reviews of the broader GABM literature have flagged credibility concerns from different angles: the underlying LLM is a black box whose biases and opacity propagate into the simulation, and prompt-engineering choices can over-determine the behavior that the agents appear to produce larooij_tornberg2026,liwu2025. Both reviews argue that this is a credibility problem for the field, but neither measures how far it goes: how sensitive a GABM result actually is to the prompt it was written with or to the LLM that produced it. Chapters 3 and 4 quantify those sensitivities, respectively.

1.3 Dissertation Structure

The three chapters are presented as stand-alone chapters that progressively narrow on the parameters governing GABM behavior: the method itself, the prompt, and the LLM.

1.3.1 Chapter 2: Epidemic Modeling with Generative Agents

Chapter 2, originally published as Williams et al. williams2023, establishes the methodology of the dissertation: the epidemic GABM. Although human behavior has long been recognized as a driver of epidemic trajectories, no prior work had used generative AI to let agents reason about and respond to an unfolding outbreak. This essay asks: to what extent can LLM-powered agents reproduce realistic human behavioral responses during an epidemic?

To address this, Chapter 2 develops the software to couple generative agents with a classical agent-based epidemic model and runs it with 1,000 agents under three feedback conditions: no feedback, self-health feedback, and combined self- and societal-health feedback. Each day, every agent is prompted with its persona and current situation and decides whether to stay home or go out; the disease then spreads through contacts among the agents who go out. The essay analyzes the resulting epidemic trajectories alongside the agents' individual decisions. Three fixed-effects logit regressions quantify how self- and societal-health information shifts the stay-home decision, and a random-effects logit examines how personality traits, age, and gender shape responses.

Chapter 2 delivers the first epidemic GABM, and shows that generative agents reproduce realistic behavior at both the individual and population levels. Individuals quarantine when symptomatic and self-isolate as community cases climb. The population exhibits multiple infection waves followed by a low, stable endemic period. Beyond the artifact itself, it shows that an LLM can stand in for hand-coded behavioral rules, opening the methodological space the following two chapters examine.

1.3.2 Chapter 3: Prompt Sensitivity of Generative Agents

Chapter 3 shifts from building the method to stress-testing it. If an LLM produces each agent’s decision from a text prompt, then the exact wording of that prompt is a modeling choice, yet no formal methodology existed for evaluating how prompt wording influences generative-agent outcomes. This essay treats the prompt as a parameter of the model and asks: how do different types of prompt modification alter agent behavior produced by the GABM?

Building on the Chapter 2 model, Chapter 3 perturbs the agent prompt along three dimensions: semantic variation (synonymous and near-synonymous rewordings), contextual shift (changing the scenario the agent faces), and persona-name substitution (probing for racial and sex bias). Each experiment runs the epidemic model five times, and the resulting mobility curves are compared with a baseline through multilinear regression with dummy variables marking the experimental condition.

The essay introduces the field’s first prompt-sensitivity analysis for generative agents, and shows that the prompt is a parameter along some dimensions but not others: synonymous rewording and persona-name swaps leave epidemic trajectories statistically unchanged, whereas minor wording changes and contextual shifts both move the results. GABM findings are therefore conditional on prompt-design choices, and GABM modelers should treat prompt-sensitivity testing as a standard part of reporting their results.

1.3.3 Chapter 4: LLM Sensitivity of Generative Agents

Chapter 4 extends the same logic from the prompt to the LLM behind it. Chapter 2 and 3 both ran on GPT-3.5; as the LLM landscape expanded to dozens of models across several providers and generations, it remained unknown whether GABM findings generalize beyond the single model that produced them. No formal methodology existed for evaluating how the choice of LLM influences generative-agent outcomes. This essay asks: how does the choice of LLM alter agent behavior produced by the GABM?

Chapter 4 holds the agents fixed and varies only the LLM. A probe spans 21 LLM configurations from OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google, crossed with 100 personas, 40 community-infection levels, and 5 repetitions, yielding 420,000 binary stay-home decisions. Three analyses test whether LLM choice affects aggregate mobility curves, persona-level effects, and the content of agent reasoning.

The essay introduces the field’s first cross-provider LLM-sensitivity analysis, and establishes that the LLM, too, is a parameter of GABM. Most models agree on the direction of persona effects but diverge several-fold in magnitude, and the attributes a researcher would normally use to choose a model (size, version, training-data cutoff, release date) fail to predict where its behavior lands. Together with Chapter 3, this reframes GABM as a method whose quantitative outputs are conditional on two new design dimensions: the prompt and the LLM, both of which should be reported and tested as standard practice.

1.4 Contributions and Impact

This dissertation makes contributions to four fields: epidemic modeling, generative AI, complexity science, and industrial and systems engineering. Across the three chapters, the contributions accumulate: Chapter 2 establishes the methodology, Chapter 3 establishes the prompt as a parameter, and Chapter 4 establishes the LLM as another parameter. Together they argue that GABM is a viable simulation method but that the prompt and the LLM are both parameters of any GABM finding.

For epidemic modeling, the dissertation introduces a framework that couples human behavior with infectious disease dynamics kermack1927. Whereas conventional epidemiological forecasts rely on hand-crafted behavioral equations and parameter estimation, the approach in Chapter 2 employs LLMs to generate agent-level decisions conditioned on the evolving state of the epidemic. This offers a fresh paradigm for epidemic modeling that captures human heterogeneity at the individual level without requiring the modeler to specify behavioral rules in advance.

For complexity science, the dissertation provides a generalizable method for embedding human decision-making within simulations of social systems. Historically, scientists have had to formulate mathematical equations and then parameterize their own behavioral models klein2018. By using generative AI, the modeler leverages an LLM to represent the human response to the state of the system. As generative AI models continue to scale, generative agent-based models are likely to capture human nuance more faithfully, improving accuracy in proxying individual-level human responses park2023.

For generative AI, the dissertation contributes to an open and pressing question: how faithfully can a language model simulate human behavior? LLMs are increasingly cast as proxies for people, and this dissertation tests that hope in a behaviorally rich setting where agents make repeated, risk-driven decisions over the course of an epidemic. The hope is partly borne out: the agents reproduce recognizably human patterns, from individual self-isolation as cases rise to multiple infection waves and an endemic tail at the population level, yet how strongly any given trait or condition shapes their behavior depends on the prompt and the LLM. The contribution is less a single verdict than a map of where the simulation holds and where it breaks.

For industrial and systems engineering, the dissertation introduces a new method for studying sociotechnical systems: generative agent-based modeling. The method is instantiated here for an epidemic (Chapter 2), where human behavior and disease transmission form a coupled feedback loop, but it extends naturally to any sociotechnical setting in which human decisions and system dynamics drive one another. Chapters 3 and 4 then evaluate the new method’s limitations, mapping where its outputs can and cannot be trusted so future scientists can use it responsibly for design and decision-making.

Together, the three chapters argue that GABM is most defensible for qualitative claims about agent behavior and weakest for the quantitative claims modelers most often want to make. Prompt sensitivity and LLM sensitivity should become routine evidentiary expectations in GABM publications.

Chapter 2: Epidemic Modeling with Generative Agents

Abstract

This study offers a new paradigm of individual-level modeling to address the grand challenge of incorporating human behavior in epidemic models. Using generative artificial intelligence in an agent-based epidemic model, each agent is empowered to make its own reasonings and decisions via connecting to a large language model such as ChatGPT. Through various simulation experiments, we present compelling evidence that generative agents mimic real-world behaviors such as quarantining when sick and self-isolation when cases rise. Collectively, the agents demonstrate patterns akin to multiple waves observed in recent pandemics followed by an endemic period. Moreover, the agents successfully flatten the epidemic curve. This study creates potential to improve dynamic system modeling by offering a way to represent human brain, reasoning, and decision making.

2.1 Introduction

The COVID-19 pandemic has emphasized the vital role of human behavior, including practices such as social distancing, wearing masks, and vaccination, in shaping the trajectory of the disease behaviour2020,kraemer2020. People's responses to perceived risks create a feedback loop that influences the pandemic’s future course, which in turn influences pandemic outcomes and future public risk perception rahmandad2022. Despite widespread recognition of the importance of incorporating changes in human behavior in epidemic models ferguson2007,bauch2013,funk2010, many modelers have yet to comprehensively address this crucial aspect funk2010,bedson2021. While some empirically grounded models have integrated past human behavior data to model disease progression, often by exogenously feeding time series data (e.g., mobility and seasonality data) xu2021, only a limited number of models have endogenously formulated the dynamic nature of human behavior as a response to the state of the disease rahmandad2021,rahmandad2022a. The lack of endogenous formulations have resulted in biases in long-term projections rahmandad2022a and hence to suboptimal policy recommendations rahmandad2021a. Advancing the inclusion of human behavior in epidemic models is, therefore, essential to improve the accuracy of projections and inform more effective policy decisions rahmandad2022.

Incorporating human behavior into epidemic models entails multiple challenges funk2015. Human behavior is complex and diverse, varying across individuals, communities, cultures, and contexts. Integrating such complexity into models poses difficulties in representation and parameterization. In addition, the lack of a unified, validated theory of human behavioral change in response to an infectious disease, and limited collaboration with behavioral and social scientists, have isolated epidemic models from adequate incorporation of behavioral mechanisms weston2020. Acquiring reliable and detailed data on behavior and validating behavioral mechanisms are also challenging verelst2016. Consequently, many epidemic models are not coupled with behavioral models or are limited in the extent to which they integrate change in human behavior rahmandad2022a. We argue that new developments in artificial intelligence (AI), specifically generative AI, are changing the behavioral modeling landscape, with major implications for epidemic modeling.

The field of generative AI has witnessed remarkable growth in recent years, revolutionizing various domains of science and research cooper2023,mondal2023,park0000. Generative AI refers to AI with the ability to generate new content, such as images, text, music, and videos, by learning patterns and structures from existing data cao0000. Generative AI models (e.g., GPT-3.5, DALL-E) are first trained on a diverse corpus of data (e.g., books, music, articles, images, and websites), thus increasing their usability and range of applications metz2023. We build on this promising idea and leverage generative AI’s power to create more realistic representations of human behavior for epidemic modeling. We hypothesize that generative AI can help incorporate human behavior in epidemic models by offering a modeling paradigm that is radically different not only from conventional compartmental models, but also from rule-based individual-level models (often referred to as agent-based models, or ABM).

In this chapter, we present a generative agent-based model (GABM) of an epidemic that incorporates changes in human behavior by utilizing generative agents as representative proxies. Unlike conventional ABMs, this new paradigm has empowered each agent by generative AI giving them the ability to reason and decide based on available information without modelers enforcing any decision-making rule—as Figure 1 illustrates. Our proposed paradigm introduces agents with distinct personas, representing society, that capture various characteristics capable of influencing human responses to an evolving epidemic from an infectious virus. Similar to residents in a town, these agents can go through daily routines (such as reading the daily news) and make decisions (such as whether to go to work). Crucially, the agents’ decisions regarding whether to go to work are influenced by their individual personalities mccrae1992, information about the virus, their own perceived health, and the perceived risks of infection derived from multiple information sources, including daily case counts reported in news outlets. Their decision-making process is powered by a large language model (LLM) such as ChatGPT. Leveraging an LLM, these agents possess realistic personas that, while varying across individuals, represent different groups of people in society.

Through several simulation experiments, we present compelling evidence that configuring our model to closely mimic real-world conditions yields agents who are risk-responsive, self-isolate as the number of cases grows, and quarantine when they feel sick. Moreover, the agents consider age-related risks and their reasoning and decisions are affected by their personalities. Collectively, the agents’ interactions result in emerging patterns akin to many real-world observations. Notably, the emerging patterns qualitatively mimic the multiple waves observed in recent pandemics followed by a period of endemicity. Our agents collectively flatten the epidemic curve and decrease the total number of cases.

This research represents a significant advance in the field of epidemiological modeling resulting from allowing AI to integrate epidemic modeling with the social and behavioral sciences. This integrated approach allows for the representation of human behavior and its interconnectedness with the spread of the disease, fostering a dynamic interaction between the two. This progress serves as a crucial step toward addressing the challenges associated with incorporating human behavior in epidemic models, with applications in many other complex social system modeling problems.

2.2 Methods

2.2.1 Conceptual Model

As Figure 1 shows, the model is a combination of two parts: GAI to model each agent’s reasoning and behavior, and ABM to model the spread of the disease. Each time step begins in the GAI component, where every agent decides whether to leave home; agents who choose to go out are then passed to the ABM component, which models their daily interactions and the disease transmission those contacts produce.

In the GAI sector, powered by ChatGPT, each agent is fed a prompt that includes the agent’s name, age, traits the agent possesses, basic biography, and relevant memories. The information comprising the relevant memories is set based on the experimental condition. For instance, it can include the agent’s symptoms (if any) or information about the percentage of sick individuals in the town (see “Experimental Design” below for details). Then, at each time step, agents are asked whether they should stay at home for the entire day and their reason. For those who decide to leave their home, the ABM portion pairs them with up to the contact rate’s worth of unique partners—scaled by how many other individuals are also out—creating opportunities for disease transmission between infected and susceptible people. Once all agent interactions are carried out, the time step increments by one, and health statuses are updated.

2.2.2 Experimental Design

Our experimental design includes three main conditions: base run, self-health feedback, and full feedback. During the base run, agents are informed about the town, their personalities and age, and the fact that they go to work to earn a living. In this condition, agents receive no information about the epidemic and no feedback of any kind, and even though they can decide whether to stay home and consequently not interact with others, we expect the model to replicate basic ABM outcomes. In the self-health feedback condition, in addition to the base run information, agents are informed about health symptoms they are experiencing, which can potentially cause them to self-quarantine by staying at home. We hypothesize that some agents will practice self-quarantine based on information about their symptoms, which should in turn decrease the infection rate. In the full feedback condition, in addition to the self-health feedback, agents read daily news reporting the percentage of townspeople newly diagnosed with the virus. We hypothesize that some agents will self-isolate as town cases rise, and that this behavioral feedback will drive the epidemic into oscillatory, multi-wave dynamics. For all three types of model simulations, each agent provides a daily decision for each time step of whether to go to work, and their reasoning behind the decision. We conduct our ten runs of n=1000 for all 3 feedback conditions with a R0 of 3.

2.3 Results

Given the computational intensiveness of this study, we first conduct our simulation experiments with a population of 1000 agents. We use the pseudonyms of Catasat and Dewberry Hollow for the virus and the town’s names, respectively, to avoid any unknown biases in LLM. When information about the virus is provided, it is specified that Catasat is an airborne human-to-human infectious virus with unknown deadliness.

Figure 2 shows the results for this population under three experimental setups, each one conducted 10 times. In the first condition (Figure 2A), there is no information feedback to the agents, and we expect the model to replicate the common observation in conventional susceptible, infectious, and recovered (SIR) models (compartmental or ABM) of a single bell-shaped curve of daily cases and constant mobility. Figure 2A plots the infection trajectories and mobility from all ten simulations alongside their respective means. The epidemic continues until almost everyone is infected. Fig 2B reports results from the second experimental condition, in which agents are informed about their own health and, based on this new information, they can alter their decision regarding whether to go outside. The results show a decline in mobility due to self-quarantine, and the subsequent decline in the maximum peak of the epidemic.

Figure 2C shows the full feedback condition, in which agents learn about the town’s cases in addition to discovering their own health-related feelings. As depicted, agents react to the growing number of cases, self-isolate, and as a result, the number of cases declines. Generative agents provided multiple reasons for choosing to stay home, including perceived higher risks and the presence of symptoms. For example, the reason Brandy (an agent) chose to stay home at a particular time is indicated as: “There is a possibility that I may have contracted the Catasat virus due to my cough and fever. Additionally, the fact that 0.9% of the population were diagnosed with new infections yesterday indicates that the virus is spreading in Dewberry Hollow. Going to work may increase my chances of getting infected or spreading the virus to others.”

Using all the data from the full feedback condition runs, Figure 2D depicts the correlation between past daily cases that were communicated with the agents and the collective decision of going out, presented by the percentage that decide to go to work. The observed relation follows a negative exponential relationship and is qualitatively consistent with several empirical studies of how humans responded to change in disease prevalence or daily death rate rahmandad2021,ghaffarzadegan2021. Lastly, panel E illustrates the average cumulative infected over time.

Generative agents who chose to leave home for work provided reasons such as the need to earn money. For example, the same agent Brandy gives the following reason for going to work at a particular time: “The infection rate in Dewberry Hollow is currently at 0.1%, which means that the risk of contracting the virus is still relatively low. Additionally, going to work is necessary for earning money to support myself” (Figure 4 gives more examples of reasons). This case demonstrates that without modelers imposing any decision-making rules on how to behave based on the available information, agents’ reasoning (which is based on an LLM) influences their decisions. Collectively, agents flatten the curve of the epidemic as they react. The different individual sample runs show the emergence of epidemic waves, which can range from a single wave to multiple waves of different sizes.

Figure 3 also compares several measures across the experiments. It shows that generative agents with information about prevalence are able to bring down the number of cumulative cases and the maximum number of cases by lowering their own mobility, while their reactions quadruple the epidemic duration.

Finally, we conduct an individual-level analysis of the agents’ decisions to stay home. Figure 4 and Table 2 report the results. Figure 4A shows a prompt sample used to prime one of the agents. Panel B demonstrates the distribution of days during which agents stayed home. In an 85-day, 1000 agent simulation, 6.4% of agents did not stay home on any days, while more than 8.4% remained at home for four days. Panel C depicts three agents’ personalities, their decisions, and a sample of their reasons for staying home or going outside, indicating agents provided diverse reasons for their decision.

To examine systematically whether health information about agents and their town influences their behaviors, we conduct several regression analyses (reported in Table 2). The dependent variable is a binary variable of an agent’s decision to stay home (equal to 1 if the agent stays home and 0 otherwise). The first three regressions are fixed effect logit models that investigate the impact of agents’ own health and societal health information on their decisions. As regression 1 shows, those with a slight cough and both a fever and cough are more likely to stay home than those who feel “normal.” The improvement in both pseudo R-squared and BIC from regression 1 and 2 shows the importance of societal health information for predicting agents’ decisions. In regression 3, adding the squared of societal health information enhances both BIC and pseudo R-squared, indicating that societal health information has a nonlinear relationship with agents’ decisions. In other words, this relationship exhibits diminishing marginal returns: while higher case counts increase the likelihood of staying home, the positive coefficient on societal health and negative coefficient on the societal health squared term reveal that each successive increase in case counts has a progressively smaller impact on the probability of isolation behavior. Regression 4, a random effect logit model, examines the impact of different personality traits, age, and gender on agents’ response. The direction of the main independent variables is consistent with regressions 1–3.

2.4 Discussions

In this chapter, we offer a new epidemic modeling approach to incorporate human behavior in infectious disease models by leveraging generative AI, specifically LLMs. In this paradigm of individual-level epidemic modeling, each agent is empowered by AI to make decisions (here, whether to go outside) by correlating different pieces of contextual information (such as their personality traits, health status, and the prevalence of the disease) with the common knowledge that exists in an LLM (here, ChatGPT). In this approach, rather than a modeler formulating decision-making rules and estimating their corresponding parameter values for each agent, she empowers each agent with reasoning power by connecting them to a well-performing LLM. We show that without the modeler imposing decision choices (i.e., decisions as exogenous inputs), or how to make decisions (i.e., decision rules), agents can make decisions that are consistent with how humans behave in the real world. For example, in our case, they are more likely to decide to self-isolate when cases rise, quarantine when they feel sick, or stay home if they have a more risk-averse personality or are elderly. Through interactions of these generative agents, two emergent behaviors are observed: the agents are collectively able to flatten the curve of the epidemic; and the system recreates various modes of an epidemic, including multiple waves and continuing endemic states. Appendix A illustrates this multi-wave behavior across ten independent full feedback runs.

We demonstrate the results in three steps by performing various simulation experiments coupled with a conventional agent-based model with an LLM through API calls. In the base-run scenario, we show that in the absence of feedback about the disease, self-health, and societal health, the generative agents behave similar to rule-based agents in classic ABM frameworks and SIR compartmental models. In the next step, informing agents about their own health at the beginning of each time step, we observe that agents with symptoms are more likely to decrease their mobility. Most agents with symptoms of a fever and cough quarantine themselves by staying home. As a result, agents are able to slow the spread of the disease. Finally, when agents are primed with societal health information, news about the epidemic, and the daily active case count in their simulated town, they are able to flatten the curve of the epidemic substantially in their town by self-isolating. Individual-level analysis shows variation in the decisions of agents and different responsiveness to news about the virus, resembling a real-world situation.

This research contributes to the literature on infectious disease modeling kermack1927 by proving a new epidemic modeling approach. While several previous studies stressed the importance of coupling disease models (such as compartmental models or ABMs), few have formally pursued this path qiu2022,rahmandad2022b. Our research contributes to this body of literature by providing a novel way for closing the feedback loop between disease and human behavior. This approach, while resonating with dynamic approaches that endogenously formulate human behavior richardson2011, differs from conventional dynamic modeling approaches by relying on LLMs to represent human responses to the state of the system rather than on explicit mathematical formulations and parameterizations of human behavior.

Beyond creating a new method of epidemic modeling, this study contributes to the literature on complexity and complex system modeling by providing a new approach to incorporating human behavior in simulation models of social systems. Identifying, formulating, and parametrizing human responses in complex systems are always challenging; in the generative agent approach, modelers can rely on LLMs to represent human response to change in the state of the system. This has the potential to change how complex systems are modeled with their human elements, leading to powerful dynamic models that more accurately represent human responses at the individual level. This accuracy comes from the fact that pre-trained AI programs are fostered by large volumes of textual data about how humans behave under similar conditions.

In addition, this work contributes to the evolving field of GAI. Over the past year, generative artificial intelligence has begun to be disseminated into all parts of society. In terms of behavioral modeling, Park and colleagues park0000 have provided a groundbreaking contribution by developing and using a memory architecture for their models. In our model, due to computational complexity, a higher number of agents, and a longer period of simulation, we had to avoid the computational intensity that comes with the use of memory. Thus, our work differs architecturally by using prompt engineering, which is equivalent to a daily reminder for each individual. Nevertheless, technological advancements and higher computational powers can facilitate the use of memory architecture in generative agents and improve GABMs.

A major limitation of this study is related to the fact that the field of generative AI is in its infancy and still developing. Our models are relatively resource-intensive, costly and relatively time-consuming to run. As of June 2023, an epidemic model of 1,000 agents can run for more than 90 hours with 32 GB of RAM CPU of program runtime costing about $20 per run, primarily due to the tens of thousands of application program interface (API) calls made to OpenAI’s servers, in addition to other hardware costs. However, as LLMs improve in cost and speed in the coming years, there is hope that GABM’s computational expense and time can be reduced. There is already some hope with the advent of local-running LLMs. We invite modelers from different disciplines— from economics, political science, sociology, and ecology to epidemiology and health policy—to explore this avenue of modeling and contribute to more realistic representations of human reasoning and behavior in complex systems.

Chapter 3: Prompt Sensitivity of Generative Agents

Abstract

As generative AI gains traction, researchers are investigating its potential to serve as proxies for humans. From undergoing cognitive psychology experiments to experiencing an epidemic, generative agents, agents powered by generative AI models, produce realistic human behavior when prompted. This study explores the sensitivity of these generative agents’ behavior to prompt modifications and varied persona names of the agents. To assess this sensitivity, we use a generative agent epidemic model, wherein each agent is prompted daily on whether it wants to isolate or commingle with other agents. We found that using synonymous prompts results in negligible changes to the model’s outcomes. However, minor variations in prompts, as well as contextual changes, do influence the model’s results. Lastly, our data indicates that different persona names assigned to generative agents, specifically those imbued with personas, do not significantly impact epidemic outcomes.

One-Sentence Summary: In an epidemic model, generative agents exhibit behavior that is insensitive to synonymous prompt and persona name modifications but have behavior that is sensitive to slight and contextual prompt modifications.

Keywords: Prompt Sensitivity, Generative Artificial Intelligence, Large Language Models, Agent Based Model, Generative Agent Based Model, Computational Social Science, ChatGPT, Sensitivity Analysis, LLM-based agents

3.1 Introduction

Over the past year, the study of generative artificial intelligence (AI) has seen significant advances openai_chatgpt2022. Generative AI refers to AI models that can create content such as text, images, videos, and music in response to user prompts. Since these models are trained on massive amounts of human-generated data brown2020, social scientists have explored their ability to roleplay as a representative proxy for human behavior.

From exploring tacit collusion han2023 to creating more humanistic thinking chatbots park2023,khattab2022,wei2022,brown2020,huang2022,wu2022,jiang2023, the possibilities of modeling human behavior using agents powered by large language models (LLM) seems increasingly likely hutson2023. A generative agent is an entity that uses LLMs to personify itself and simulate realistic human behavior park2023. To interact with the generative agent, the author feeds text prompts as inputs to the LLM han2023,park2023,williams2023,akata2023,phelps2023. The LLM then proceeds to generate text responses as outputs brown2020. In some applications of generative AI, the survey-like experiment ends there, and the output responses are analyzed binz2023,horton2023,dillion2023,argyle2023,hamilton2023. For other experiments, researchers have conducted dynamic experiments with generative agents on social science models such as information diffusion park2023 and epidemic models williams2023 to test their believability as proxies for human behavior.

For the information diffusion model, generative agents are placed in a virtual, pixelated world to go about their daily lives park2023,openai_chatgpt2022. In this seminal paper, generative agents have names and traits; together, the agents make up a small-town named Smallville. In this community, an agent can interact with other agents, the environment, and even itself. The agent recalls its actions and conversations through a memory architecture, which in turn helps the agent plan and act in future time steps wang2023. To test information diffusion in this simulation, the authors implanted a thought in a single agent of throwing a Valentine’s Day party. With time, the agent—as well as other invited guests—spread the word of the Valentine’s Day party, resulting in many agents attending the event.

While the information diffusion model is a proof-of-concept that generative agents can operate in social-science experiments, the epidemic model quantitatively studies an epidemic in Dewberry Hollow, a virtual town full of generative agents williams2023. Here, agents are informed daily about their own health as well as the fictional virus spreading within their community. Feeding this information in the form of a prompt to ChatGPT, these agents, personified with the Big Five traits, make decisions on whether they want to isolate or commingle. These individuals’ decisions exhibited heterogeneous risk-tolerances, and collectively, their behavior creates multiple waves of an epidemic.

Other dynamic models incorporating generative agents have also begun to emerge. In some game theory applications, generative agents compete against each other in the Prisoner’s Dilemma akata2023,phelps2023 as well as the Battle of the Sexes akata2023. For the study of social networks, researchers have agents dynamically react to each other's posts and comments in a Reddit-like setting park2022. Like the Reddit paper, another model named S3 creates a social-network as well as realistic human-personas from data-sources gao2023. Furthermore, they calibrate the agent-to-agent social media interactions to replicate information diffusion found on social media for two events: the Japan Nuclear Waste Water Release Event and the Chained Eight-child Mother event.

Regardless of whether the generative agent application is static or dynamic, all these models lack one thing: robust sensitivity analysis. As LLMs have grown in popularity, concerns about their sensitivity to text-input have emerged gekhman2023,zhuo2023. However, according to Wang et al.’s comprehensive literature review of generative agents, there is no unified way to prompt LLMs nor to conduct prompt sensitivity analysis wang2023. Compared to traditional sensitivity analysis, generative agent sensitivity analysis must have prompt sensitivity analysis in addition to regular parameter sensitivity analysis. Prompt sensitivity analysis has a significantly larger dimensional compared to traditional numeric sensitivity analysis space due to the infinite possibilities of linguistic expression that can convey the same meaning. Given that LLMs are designed to predict the next word in a sequence, it is critical that variations in prompts do not drastically change the generative agent's behavior. Concerns about sensitivity of prompt modifications stem from researchers post-processing LLM text outputs to determine the decision the generative agent shall take park2023,williams2023,park2022,gao2023; hence, if a minor prompt change impacts the agent decision, the results from the model would not be robust. Therefore, it is vital to assess the sensitivity of these LLMs to different input prompts to understand how it influences the generative agents’ behavioral response.

This chapter is a step toward generative agent sensitivity analysis. As it is impossible to explore the entire prompt sensitivity space for an LLM, we will use the generative agent epidemic model as an example model to test prompt sensitivity williams2023. This model was selected amongst other generative agent models for two distinct reasons. First, this model has a mostly non-changing vignette-style prompt fed into a LLM each time step for each agent. Comparatively, some models, like the Valentine’s Day information diffusion model, have generative agents with memory architecture, which in turn creates more input prompt variability han2023,park2023,park2022,gao2023. Therefore, the epidemic model prompt helps reduce the potential amount of prompt dimensionality juxtaposed with other models. Secondly, the epidemic model has an easy to measure impact from the prompt: the resulting mobility curve. Mobility curves (seen in Figure 6 and Figure 7) compare the mobility of people at the current time step to the percent of new active case infections that happened the previous time step. If the prompt is sensitive to alterations, then the resulting mobility curve will have a different shape as well as values. To comprehensively analyze the robustness of the prompt williams2023, three types of prompt sensitivity analysis will be conducted: semantic variation sensitivity, contextual shift sensitivity, and persona name sensitivity.

Semantic variation sensitivity analysis modifies parts of the prompt by substituting them with terms or phrases that are similar semantically to assess any resulting changes in the LLM’s response. When using synonymous phrases in prompts (e.g., “knows about” and “is aware of”), we hypothesize that the agent’s behavior will not change; thereby, not altering the mobility curve. In contrast, we hypothesize that using nonsynonymous phrasing (e.g., “knows” and “learns”) will result in different agent behavior.

Contextual shift sensitivity analysis involves altering the scenario the generative agent faces by modifying the prompt. To test the epidemic model’s contextual shift sensitivity, we examine if agents, facing less fiscal pressure, react differently compared to the baseline prompt. We hypothesize that the resulting agent’s behavior will change as the agents confront a different scenario.

Lastly, for persona name sensitivity analysis, generative agents’ names are substituted for other names to evaluate the differences in the LLM’s response based on name change (e.g. “Betty” versus “Jamal”). A major concern that researchers have when leveraging AI models is perpetuating racial and sex biases zou2018. In 2015, Amazon shut down its recruiting AI tool as it was found to be biased against women dastin2018. Similarly, in 2020, Google apologized for its Vision AI model mislabeling objects as guns held by darker-skinned individuals, while identifying them as different objects for lighter-skinned individuals kayserbril2020. Hence, it is vital to ensure LLMs are not biased by generative agents’ names: names which may subtly hint at sex and race. We hypothesize that when generative agents have personality traits, such as the Big Five personality traits, their names are not a significant factor. The logic is that human-traits will be the primary driver of how the generative agent acts versus that of their name.

Through several experiments, we present compelling evidence and statistically significant results. For semantic variation sensitivity analysis, we find that synonymous phrasing produces no statistically significant deviations from the baseline. For nonsynonymous phrasing, statistically significant deviations were noted. Similarly, our contextual shift sensitivity analysis showed a statistically significant different mobility curve than the baseline. Lastly, all tests conducted for name sensitivity analysis resulted in no statistically significant deviation from the baseline results.

3.2 Materials and Methods

3.2.1 Background Model Information

Since the analysis of this chapter relies on Williams et al.’s model, this section provides necessary background information of their generative agent-based epidemic model as well as a summary of some of their findings williams2023. At the start of each time step in the epidemic model, each generative agent is asked whether it wants to stay at home. To arrive at this decision, each agent is provided a prompt (Figure 5, Panel A) with static information, such as their name, age, personality traits, and job status. The prompt also contains dynamic information about the agent’s health and the percentage of new active cases detected in the community during the previous time step. After all the information is supplied, ChatGPT functions as the agent’s decision-making ability, responding with either “yes” or “no” to the question of whether the agent wants to stay at home. If the agent opts to stay at home, it will not have any encounters with other agents during that time step. Conversely, if the agent decides to venture outside, it will interact with other agents. Contacts between infected and susceptible agents result in a probabilistic chance that the susceptible agent becomes infected.

Using this algorithm, the epidemic model produces intriguing outcomes at both individual and societal levels. In simulations that incorporated self-health feedback, many agents displaying symptoms such as fever and cough chose to quarantine, thereby slowing the spread of the epidemic. When the model included both self and societal health feedback, agents not only self-quarantined but also chose to isolate when the community reported lots of new active cases. This behavior contributed to flattening the epidemic curve through self-isolation. The agents, with various traits, exhibited varied levels of risk-responsiveness, leading to heterogenous behaviors. The collective actions of these agents resulted in multiple waves of the virus and ongoing endemic states.

We use the self and societal health feedback prompt for our prompt sensitivity analysis due to its ability to elicit a diverse set of reasonings from the agents: not only do some ill agents provide reasons to quarantine, but risk-responsive agents also justify their decisions to isolate williams2023. Since the primary goal of the generative agent epidemic model is to integrate human behavior into epidemic models, it is essential agents are informed about the virus, which only occurs when both self and societal health feedback prompt.

3.2.2 Experimental Design

We ran three types of prompt sensitivity analysis: semantic variation, contextual shift, and persona name sensitivity analysis. Semantic variation sensitivity analysis modifies portions of the prompt by replacing them with semantically similar terms or phrases to assess any resulting changes in the LLM’s response. Contextual shift sensitivity analysis also modifies portions of the prompt, but with the goal of revising the scenario the generative agent faces. Lastly, persona name sensitivity analysis substitutes the generative agents’ names to investigate whether the LLM has any racial or sex biases. Table 3 shows each experiment tested for each type of sensitivity analysis.

For semantic variation sensitivity analysis, only the prompt in the epidemic model code was altered majumdar_gabm_epidemic. To test for semantic sensitivity, Figure 5’s panel B and C exhibit the semantic modifications that we did to the epidemic model’s base prompt, seen in panel A. To test for synonymous semantic sensitivity, experiment #1 replaces the words “knows about” (Figure 5, Panel A) with “is aware of” (Figure 5, Panel B). For nonsynonymous semantic variation, experiment #2 removes the word “knows” and replaces it with the word “learns” (Figure 5, Panel C). Although the two words seem similar, “learns” implies a recently completed journey of understanding while “knows” refers to having already acquired the knowledge. Therefore, we hypothesize that agents with “learns” will be more risk adverse given their novel familiarity with the virus, thereby decreasing the mobility curve values; this means there will be less mobility for an equivalent percentage of new active case infections when compared to the baseline.

Like the semantic variation experiments, contextual shift sensitivity analysis only modifies the prompt in the code. In Figure 5, experiment #3 has the line “[agent’s name] goes to work to earn money to support [agent’s name]” (Figure 5, Panel A) substituted for “even though [agent’s name] already has enough savings, [agent’s name] goes to work to earn money (Figure 5, Panel D).” The goal of this statement is to alter the environmental circumstance the agent faces; knowing it has the financial means to stay at home, the agent may be less inclined to go to work. Therefore, we hypothesize the agents will be much more risk adverse as they do not have the fiscal incentive to go to work.

In persona name sensitivity analysis, the prompt remains untouched, but the method to generative names has changed. Experiment #4 assesses sex biases in the LLM. Instead of employing the 50 most popular male and female American names, it uses the 100 most popular female American names. Experiment #5 evaluates the impact of specific individual names. For this, all 100 agents are assigned top male and female names: 50 agents are named Jose, while the remaining 50 are named Maria.

Apart for the experiment changes listed in Table 3, the code closely adheres to the original generative agent epidemic model, though with some adjustment. Consistent with the Williams et al. paper, every run in this study involves 100 agents: 98 susceptible and 2 initially infected. Other parameters are set as follows: the time to heal is 6 days, the contact rate is 5, infectivity is 0.1, and the max number of time steps is 50. However, diverging from the Williams et al. methodology, for the semantic variation and contextual shift sensitivity analyses, we will select the agents’ names from the top 100 most popular American names (50 male and 50 female) instead of the top 200 most popular American names (100 male and 100 female). For our study, each experiment will be run 5 times.

Figure 6 and Figure 7 display the resulting mobility curves from each experiment. To discern differences in these mobility curves relative to the baseline, we employ multilinear regression. The outcomes are presented in Table 4. Here, mobility—the percentage of agents choosing to leave home—is the dependent variable as a function of new active daily cases, new active daily cases squared, and a variable called experiment. Experiment is a dummy variable; when it is set to 1 if the data is from the experiment being tested and equals 0 if the data is from the baseline. For each experiment, two regressions are run: the first regression just has an experiment constant while the second regression has an experimental constant and an additional interaction variable between experiment and the number of new cases. A statistically significant β3 or β4 suggests that the experiment has a measurable effect on the resulting mobility curve relative to the baseline.

From the results in Table 4, the multilinear regression shows that experiment #1, #4 and #5 are not significant. Therefore, synonymous semantic variations as well as the persona name sensitivity modifications did not impact the model’s outcome. For nonsynonymous semantic variation, experiment #2 illustrates differences in semantics matter. Moreover, our hypothesis is affirmed: agents who “learns” about the epidemic are more risk-adverse than the baseline. For contextual shift sensitivity analysis, experiment #3 reaffirms that changing the scenario agents face does result in a different mobility curve. However, unlike our hypothesis, agents who have “enough savings” display less risk aversion. Rather than living off their savings, these agents are more inclined to work compared to those who must support themselves.

3.4 Discussions

In this chapter, we conduct prompt sensitivity analysis for generative agent-based modeling. We explored three different types of sensitivity analysis: semantic variation, contextual shift, and persona name sensitivity analysis. For the semantic variation sensitivity analysis, we demonstrated that exchanging synonymous phrases in a prompt, “knows about” and “is aware of”, led to no discernible change in the mobility curve. However, we observed that nonsynonymous semantic variations do impact the results of the model: the “learns” run exhibits reduced mobility compared to the “knows” run.

When we made changes to the prompt that shifted the scenario face by the agent, we noted departures from baseline behavior. Contrary to our initial hypothesis, the contextual shift sensitivity analysis resulted in more mobile agents. It is unclear why ChatGPT deduced that individuals who opt to work, despite having enough savings, remain more mobile during an epidemic. One hypothesis is that the LLM assumes that agents who already go to work at their own volition, without fiscal imperative, do in fact enjoy going to work. Alternatively, the savings may simply make getting sick less costly: an agent with money to fall back on can better absorb the consequences of falling ill, so it has less need to stay home, whether or not it enjoys work.

Our persona name sensitivity analysis indicated that agents’ names did not statistically influence the model’s results. This finding supports our earlier assertion that persona names, when coupled with personality traits, do not have a major influence on the epidemic outcomes simulated by our model.

Given these findings, this research contributes to the literature on generative agent-based modeling, introducing a first step towards prompt sensitivity analysis wang2023. While traditional agent-based models have long emphasized the importance of sensitivity analysis epstein2007, the advent of generative agent-based modeling necessitates new methodologies to address the unique challenges of exploring the dimensional space of prompt sensitivity. The findings of this chapter have the potential to influence how other modelers approach prompting generative agents and prompt sensitivity analysis in their generative agent-based models.

Beyond impacting the field of generative agent-based modeling, this study also contributes to the field of epidemiology through investigating the robustness of the generative agent epidemic model. Before our work, this model had no sensitivity analysis regarding its prompt. Now, we know prompt changes in Williams et al.’s model affect the results. However, the general behavior responding to cases remains robust. Hence, epidemiological modelers may have more confidence in the base model.

This study has several limitations. First, a significant limitation of this study is that it is impossible to have a prompt sensitivity analysis that encompasses all potential modifications to the prompt. For example, our synonymous test used a single, very close pair (“knows about” versus “is aware of”); more distant synonyms could still shift agent behavior. Although there have been attempts to robustly test generative agent prompts ghaffarzadegan2023, more prompt sensitivity probing can always be done. To fill this gap, future research can investigate a more systematic, comprehensive list of different types of prompt sensitivity analysis that should be performed. This way, researchers can execute specific types of prompt sensitivity tests instead of heuristically examining the feasible prompt space. Exacerbating this limitation, is the fact that generative agent-based models are resource-intensive. As of June 2023, executing a model of 100 agents across 50-time steps can take over 2 hours of program runtime on a 32GB RAM CPU and incurs a cost of approximately $2 per run. This cost stems from the thousands of application program interface calls made to OpenAI’s servers. Compared to traditional agent-based models, the scalability of a prompt sensitivity analysis can become prohibitively expensive. However, as LLMs become more efficient and affordable in the future, the fiscal, computational, and temporal resources of generative agent-based modeling will become less burdensome. Another limitation is that the results may be specific to ChatGPT, and possibly only to that specific version of ChatGPT. Future studies can investigate whether these findings apply beyond ChatGPT to other LLMs.

Overall, this study, by performing prompt sensitivity analysis on a generative agent epidemic model, has demonstrated the robustness of generative AIs’ capability to guide dynamic models of human systems. This chapter’s findings may be used as practical guidance for those working with generative agent-based models, particularly when crafting prompts for generative agents. Looking forward, we hope our results serve as a guiding light for future researchers, aiding them in prompting and in formulating more robust ways to conduct prompt sensitivity analysis for generative agent-based modeling.

Chapter 4: LLM Sensitivity of Generative Agents

Abstract

Generative agent-based modeling (GABM) replaces the rule-based decision-making of classical agent-based models with LLM-mediated decisions, enabling researchers to simulate human behavior that resists mathematical specification. The original epidemic GABM and its follow-on prompt-sensitivity study both used GPT-3.5, leaving the generalizability of GABM findings to other LLMs untested. We address this with a frozen-task probe study: 100 personas, 40 community-infection levels, and 5 repetitions across 21 LLM configurations from OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google, totaling 420,000 binary stay-home decisions during a simulated epidemic. Most LLMs agree on the direction of persona effects, but magnitudes vary several-fold by provider: largest in Gemini agents, intermediate in Claude, smallest in GPT. The model attributes a researcher would typically use to choose an LLM (size, generation, knowledge cutoff, release date) give no insight into where any given configuration’s behavior lands. The reasoning text accompanying each decision encodes substantive predictive signal beyond the decision itself, and reveals an other-focused-vs-self-focused framing of the stay-home choice. We argue that LLM choice is itself a parameterization of agent behavior: GABM with general-purpose LLMs is most defensible for qualitative claims about agent behavior and weakest for the quantitative claims modelers most often want to make. LLM sensitivity should become a routine evidentiary expectation in GABM publications.

One-Sentence Summary: In a generative-agent epidemic model, the choice of LLM is itself a parameter of agent behavior: the direction of persona effects is largely shared across LLMs, but their magnitude varies several-fold by provider.

4.1 Introduction

Generative agent-based modeling (GABM) was introduced as a paradigm for incorporating human behavior into simulations of complex social systems ghaffarzadegan2024. Where a traditional agent-based model relies on the modeler to write decision rules and tune their parameters, a GABM connects each agent to a large language model (LLM) and lets the LLM decide the agent’s actions. This sidesteps a long-standing barrier: human behavior in many domains resists clean mathematical specification, and a modeler’s hand-coded rules typically capture only a thin slice of what real people consider when making a decision.

In the two years since GABM was introduced, the LLM landscape has expanded dramatically: dozens of models span three major providers, multiple generations within each provider, distinct training-data eras, and configurable parameters such as reasoning level. As these models have scaled, they have acquired emergent reasoning and behavioral capabilities that smaller predecessors lacked — generalizing arithmetic wei_emergent2022, performing multi-step inference wei_cot2022, and learning new tasks from in-context examples brown2020 — only above certain training thresholds. As the LLMs that drive generative agents have evolved across this period, have they converged on consistent simulated behavior, or does the LLM choice itself remain a parameter of any GABM result?

The answer determines what GABM as a method can credibly claim. If LLM choice does not materially shape simulated behavior, LLMs are interchangeable and GABM is a robust technique that can be applied regardless of the model a researcher selects. If LLM choice does shape simulated behavior, every published GABM result is implicitly conditional on the LLM that produced it — complicating the method’s foundational claim that LLM decision-making removes the explicit behavioral parameterization characteristic of classical agent-based modeling epstein2007.

Two recent peer-reviewed reviews of GABM have raised convergent concerns about the field’s credibility. Larooij and Törnberg larooij_tornberg2026 argue that the integration of LLMs may exacerbate rather than resolve ABM’s long-standing challenges of empirical validation, given the black-box structure, stochastic outputs, and cultural biases of language models. Li and Wu liwu2025 argue that prompt-engineering choices in current GABM studies over-control simulation outcomes to the point that emergent behavior often reflects researcher design rather than agent dynamics. Both reviews flag the LLM as a source of methodological concern, but neither systematically measures how LLM choice propagates into simulated agent behavior. Recent surveys of the GABM literature wang2023,mou2024,gao2024hss catalog the proliferation of applications while treating the LLM as a black-box backend whose specific identity often does not enter the analytical frame. A growing body of work probes LLM-driven agents across many models in survey dominguez_olmedo2024,mooney2025, game-theoretic einwiller2025, and forecasting parikh2026 settings, but none has been conducted inside a GABM-style decision loop.

To address this empirical gap, we revisit the GABM epidemic model of Williams et al. williams2023 as our probe domain. The setting is well-suited to systematic probing across LLMs: the agent decision is binary (stay home or go out), allowing clean statistical comparison across models; each decision is accompanied by a free-text reasoning passage, enabling content analysis alongside quantitative measurement; agent personas vary along the Big Five trait dimensions, supporting persona-level regression at the individual-decision level; and the model is itself a published GABM whose sensitivity to LLM choice is precisely the kind of question this study seeks to answer.

Two prior studies have used this model. Williams et al. williams2023 introduced it on GPT-3.5, demonstrating that generative agents could produce realistic individual decisions and emergent population-level dynamics, including multiple infection waves and an endemic period. Williams williams_prompt2023 then characterized the model’s sensitivity to prompt design — how different expressions, names, and situations shape agent behavior. The present study characterizes its sensitivity to the LLM itself.

The probe design holds the same 100 personas (name, age, and Big Five trait combination) and 40 community-infection levels (0% to 7%) constant across 21 LLM configurations from three providers, prompting each agent at each infection level 5 times to account for LLM stochasticity, yielding 420,000 binary decisions. The 21 configurations span six lenses for comparison across LLMs: provider, reasoning level, model size within a family, model evolution across releases, knowledge cutoff, and release date. Each response includes a free-text reasoning passage alongside the binary decision, allowing both quantitative comparison of decision distributions and content analysis of how the agents reason. We analyze the responses through three progressively deeper analyses: aggregate mobility curves at the configuration level, persona-level random-effects logistic regressions that test how each persona attribute shapes the binary decision, and content analysis of the reasoning passages.

The investigation is organized around fourteen research questions, grouped by the three analyses. Table 5 lists each question alongside the section in which its results are presented and the figures that bear on it.

Across the 21 configurations, the results indicate most LLMs agree on the direction of persona effects but disagree several-fold on magnitude. None of the model attributes of an LLM — size, generation, knowledge cutoff, release date — give any insight into where the configuration’s behavior lands. The reasoning text encodes substantive predictive signal beyond the decision itself. We argue these findings reframe what GABM with general-purpose LLMs can credibly claim: the method is well-suited to qualitative claims about agent behavior, but the LLM choice itself functions as a parameterization for any quantitative claim. Over this two-year LLM evolution arc, generative-agent behavior has not converged.

4.2 Background & Related Work

4.2.1 Generative agent-based modeling

In a generative agent-based modeling (GABM) simulation, agents’ decisions are produced by an LLM rather than by hand-coded behavioral rules ghaffarzadegan2024. At every step the LLM is prompted with the agent’s persona and the current state of the simulation. Then, the LLM’s response is parsed into a structured action that updates that state for the next step. Ghaffarzadegan et al. formalized this loop as a methodology for combining LLM-driven decisions with classical agent-based modeling ghaffarzadegan2024.

Existing GABMs span a range of domains. Williams et al. modeled an epidemic in which agents decide each day whether to stay home or go out williams2023, a study cited as a canonical GABM example in subsequent reviews lu_aleta_moreno2024,adornetto2025. Wu et al. introduce Smart Agent-Based Modeling, a general GABM framework demonstrated across economics, finance, and engineering case studies wu_sabm2023. Vezhnevets et al. propose Concordia, a library for grounding GABM actions in physical, social, and digital spaces using a game-master that mediates state transitions vezhnevets_concordia2023. Lan et al. deploy LLM agents as players in the social-deduction game Avalon, observing emergent collaboration, confrontation, leadership, persuasion, and camouflage behaviors across multi-round gameplay lan_avalon2024. Across these systems the common pattern is the same: many agents, many timesteps, environment-coupled decisions, with collective dynamics emerging from individual LLM-driven choices.

4.2.2 LLM social simulation

A broader literature uses LLMs as social-science instruments without embedding them inside a multi-step GABM loop. Park et al.’s generative-agents architecture introduced the memory-stream, reflection, and planning pattern in a virtual-town sandbox of 25 agents pursuing daily goals and exchanging messages park2023. Gao et al.’s S3 social-network simulator deploys LLM agents that observe content, adjust attitudes, and produce posts, reproducing empirical patterns of emotion and attitude propagation around two news events gao_s3_2023. Akata et al. have LLM agents play repeated games against one another to study cooperation akata2023. Argyle et al. introduced “algorithmic fidelity” as the property by which prompted LLMs reproduce demographic-conditional opinion distributions argyle2023. Horton showed that LLM agents exhibit behaviors consistent with classical economic-agent assumptions horton2023. Binz and Schulz applied cognitive-psychology paradigms to GPT-3 and found human-like response patterns alongside human-like biases binz_schulz2023. These studies provide individual-level behavioral evidence for the use of LLMs in social-science settings.

A separate line of recent work develops methods for raising the fidelity with which an LLM reproduces a specific human or human population. Park et al.’s 1,000-personas study prompts agents with detailed interview transcripts from real participants to align each agent with its human counterpart park2024_1000. Kolluri et al. fine-tune LLMs directly on human-behavior traces from social-science experiments kolluri2025. Efforts such as Simile.ai go further still, training purpose-built models for population-level diversity rather than the single polished persona of a chat assistant simile_ai. The present study uses general-purpose chat models with light persona prompts; whether richer prompting, fine-tuning, or purpose-built models reduce the model-to-model variability documented here is left as future work.

4.2.3 LLM evaluations

LLMs are routinely evaluated on standardized benchmarks of capability — multi-domain knowledge (MMLU mmlu_hendrycks2021), program synthesis (HumanEval humaneval_chen2021), broad task suites (BIG-Bench bigbench_srivastava2022) — and on large-N head-to-head rankings via crowdsourced human preference (Chatbot Arena chatbot_arena_chiang2024). These evaluations measure what an LLM can do in isolation. They have established that frontier models differ measurably from earlier or smaller predecessors along most capability dimensions, with several capabilities emerging only above particular scale thresholds wei_emergent2022.

A second strand of work uses shallow behavioral probes — short, persona-conditioned tasks — to test how the LLM affects an agent’s behavior. Dominguez-Olmedo et al. dominguez_olmedo2024 administer multiple-choice survey instruments to 43 LLMs and report that apparent cross-model differences in opinion distributions are largely artifacts of response-ordering bias; once corrected, models collapse toward near-uniform random responding regardless of size or training corpus. Einwiller et al. einwiller2025 run a matched dictator-game probe across multiple LLMs with systematic prompt variations and find substantial cross-model divergence in giving rates and prompt sensitivity. Mooney et al. mooney2025 generate roughly five thousand demographic agents and test behavioral coherence across nine models, finding that while surface-level survey-style responses align broadly with each agent’s stated profile, behavior in subsequent agent-to-agent interactions departs from that profile, and both model family and model size shape how often the coherence breaks down. Parikh et al. parikh2026 compare nine LLM configurations against polling data from the 2024 U.S. presidential election and report systematic, model-specific divergence in favorability predictions. These studies establish that LLM choice can move behavioral output measurably.

The present study is another such probe, applied specifically to the persona-conditioned decision that feeds an epidemic GABM. The same 100 personas are presented at 40 infection levels to 21 LLM configurations, holding the prompt, persona, and scenario fixed, varying only the underlying model. Where capability benchmarks ask what an LLM can do and behavioral-fidelity experiments ask whether it acts like a human, the analysis here asks how much the LLM shapes the agent’s stay-or-go decision at the moment a GABM would query its agent. LLMs that perform similarly on capability benchmarks may still produce different mobility curves for the same population.

4.3 Methods

4.3.1 Simulation setup

The probe is a simplified extension of the generative agent-based model of Williams et al. williams2023. Two simplifications make each response cleanly attributable to the agent’s persona and the perceived infection level. First, each query is treated as an independent, one-shot decision: where Williams et al. simulated disease spread within an agent population over many days, the present probe samples each agent and infection level directly, with no inter-agent contagion. Second, agent health is held constant — each agent is told “You feel healthy and well.” — rather than evolving with a disease state, so behavioral variation reflects persona and the perceived community-infection rate alone. The town setting (Dewberry Hollow), occupational role, and disease (the fictional Catasat virus) carry over from Williams et al.

4.3.2 Agent population

The probe uses 100 frozen personas generated by the procedure of Williams et al. williams2023. The five Big Five trait labels john_srivastava1999 are each sampled independently with 50/50 probability between the dimension’s two opposing labels: extraverted or introverted (Extraversion); agreeable or antagonistic (Agreeableness); conscientious or unconscientious (Conscientiousness); emotionally stable or neurotic (Neuroticism); and open to experience or closed to experience (Openness). Ages are drawn between 18 and 65 from a probability distribution that mirrors the 2023 U.S. working-age population popclock. Names and gender are paired by sampling without replacement from the top male and female U.S. names, balanced to produce 50 female and 50 male agents. The full procedure is run with a fixed random seed (42) for reproducibility. Sample demographics are summarized in Figure 8.

4.3.3 Probe design

Each agent is queried at 40 infection levels with 5 repetitions for each combination of agent and infection level. Infection levels are sampled densely in the low range, with 0.1 percentage-point steps from 0% to 3.5%, then larger steps at 4%, 5%, 6%, and 7%. Each LLM configuration therefore produces 100 × 40 × 5 = 20,000 responses; across 21 configurations the full corpus is 420,000 responses.

4.3.4 LLM configurations

The 21 configurations were chosen to span six lenses for comparison across the contemporary LLM landscape: provider, reasoning level, model size, model evolution, knowledge cutoff, and release date. Provider is the company that built and offers the LLM; different providers train models on different data corpora, with different alignment techniques, fine-tuning regimes, and architectural choices. Reasoning level is a model-side configuration parameter that controls how much hidden deliberation the model performs before producing its final response; higher levels (off → low → medium → high) typically increase response quality and latency at the cost of more output tokens wei_cot2022. Model size is the relative scale tier of a model within a single provider’s family of releases — for example, Haiku, Sonnet, and Opus in Anthropic’s nomenclature; Lite and Flash in Google’s. Exact parameter counts and training compute are not publicly disclosed for these closed-API models. Model evolution is the sequence of major releases within a single provider’s family over time — for example, GPT-3.5 Turbo, GPT-4o, GPT-4.1, GPT-5.1, and GPT-5.2 from OpenAI; successive releases typically incorporate new training data, refined alignment, and architectural improvements. Knowledge cutoff is the date through which the model’s training data extends; models with later cutoffs have been exposed to more recent text from the public internet and other training corpora. Release date is the date the model became publicly available via API, independent of knowledge cutoff: a model can be released months or years after the cutoff date of its training data.

To support the reasoning-level lens, GPT-5.2 and Gemini 3 Flash Preview were each run at four reasoning levels (off, low, medium, high), with each level treated as a separate configuration; all other models were run with reasoning off (or, for OpenAI’s o3, with reasoning required by the API). Data collection across the 21 configurations cost approximately $840 in total API charges (~$464 OpenAI, ~$263 Anthropic, ~$113 Google). Across providers, the configurations span 9 from OpenAI, 5 from Anthropic, and 7 from Google; the full list is given in Table 6.

Sampling temperature is set to 0 for non-reasoning configurations where the API allows it; reasoning-capable models use a temperature of 1 (required by Anthropic; recommended by Google). The OpenAI o-series accepts no temperature parameter.

4.3.5 Prompt template

Every query uses the same template, identical across the 21 configurations. Each query substitutes four variables into the template: the agent’s first name, the agent’s age in years, the agent’s comma-separated Big Five trait list, and the day’s perceived community-infection rate. The full template is Figure 9.

4.3.6 Response collection and parsing

Each response is required to be a free-text reasoning passage followed by a single-word yes/no decision, in the format specified at the end of the prompt template (Figure 9). A parser extracts the two components and rejects any response that does not conform; 0 of 420,000 responses required rejection. The reasoning text is preserved for the downstream textual analysis.

4.3.7 Analytical methods

We perform three analyses on the 420,000 responses, each progressively deeper into the data: (1) Mobility Curve Analysis summarizes each LLM configuration’s aggregate behavior by plotting mobility against infection level and compares curves across configurations with multilinear regression; (2) Persona Analysis examines the agent-level predictors of the stay-home decision (Big Five trait labels, gender, age, and infection level) via two logistic regressions; and (3) Response Analysis investigates the content of the reasoning passage along three dimensions: which concepts the LLM invokes, via mention counts of curated keyword and concept lists; whether mentioning a concept amplifies its behavioral effect, via a third logistic regression that incorporates mention flags as predictors; and how agent-specific the reasoning is, via embedding-based cosine similarity.

4.3.7.1 Mobility Curve Analysis

For each LLM configuration, the stay-home rate at a given infection level is computed by averaging 500 binary decisions — 5 repetitions for each of the 100 agents. The mobility curve plots mobility (the complement of the stay-home rate, mobility = 1 − stay-home rate) on the y-axis against the 40 sampled infection levels (0 to 3.5% in 0.1 percentage-point steps, plus 4%, 5%, 6%, and 7%) on the x-axis.

To discern differences between mobility curves, we employ multilinear regression in the same style as Williams williams_prompt2023. For each set of configurations being compared (e.g., the four GPT-5.2 reasoning levels), we fit two models. Both take mobility as the dependent variable and use the infection level, the infection level squared, and one dummy variable per non-baseline configuration as predictors; the dummy is set to 1 if the data is from the configuration being tested and 0 if it is from the baseline. The first model, a baseline-shift model, stops there; a statistically significant dummy coefficient indicates that the configuration produces a level shift in the mobility curve relative to the baseline. The second model, a baseline-plus-slope-shift model, additionally includes interactions between each dummy and the infection level; a statistically significant interaction indicates that the configuration’s slope of response to infection level differs from the baseline’s. Per-comparison tables for both models are reported in Appendix D.

4.3.7.2 Persona Analysis

To examine how agent traits, gender, age, and infection level systematically influence the stay-home decision, two logistic regressions are fit per LLM configuration on the 20,000 individual responses, in the same style as Williams et al. williams2023. The dependent variable is a binary variable of the agent’s decision to stay home (equal to 1 if the agent stays home and 0 otherwise). The fixed-effect logit absorbs each agent’s average tendency to stay home through agent dummies, isolating how the decision responds to changes in infection level. It also provides a pseudo-R² for goodness-of-fit. The random-effect logit replaces the agent dummies with a single random intercept per agent, freeing up the model to estimate trait, gender, age, and infection effects. The reference categories are female, introverted, antagonistic, unconscientious, neurotic, and closed to experience; age is a continuous predictor in years. Per-configuration coefficient tables for both specifications are reported in Appendix E.1; diagnostics — calibration, residual Q-Q, random-intercept normality, BLUPs versus predictors, and residual fit by infection and age — are in Appendix F.

4.3.7.3 Response Analysis

Each query asks the agent to provide its reasoning before stating its yes/no decision, so the reasoning text likely shapes the decision rather than merely justifying it after the fact. From this text we measure four things in turn: (1) how often LLMs mention specific traits in their reasoning, via keyword counting; (2) whether those mentions amplify the corresponding behavioral effect, via the mention-flag logit; (3) how broader concept categories correlate with the decision direction, via keyword counting; and (4) how agent-specific the reasoning text is, via embedding cosine similarity.

For each Big Five trait label, a keyword set covers the obvious word-form variants of each pole. For example, an extraversion mention is flagged when any of {extroverted, extrovert, extraverted, extravert, extraversion, extroversion, introverted, introvert, introversion} appears in the response. Two additional flags cover infection (keywords like {infection, infected, cases, diagnosed} or any number followed by “%” or “percent”) and age (keywords like {years old, young, age, old}, or the agent’s own age number). All flags are binary per response and case-insensitive.

These mention flags then enter a third logistic regression — the mention-flag logit — which extends the random-effects logit from §4.3.7.2 with one binary indicator per Big Five trait label, plus one for infection and one for age. A flag enters the model only when its overall mention rate falls within [15%, 85%]; outside this window the flag carries no usable variation. The mention-flag coefficients answer whether mentioning a concept amplifies or dampens its behavioral effect. Per-configuration coefficient tables are in Appendix E.2; diagnostics are in Appendix F.

A separate, broader concept dictionary covers five themes: Work, Community, Self Health, Virus Properties, and Virus Prevalence. These categories were drawn from the prompt template (Figure 9), which mentions going to the office for work, feeling healthy and well, an airborne virus that spreads from human to human, a daily new-infection rate, and the town of Dewberry Hollow. Keywords for each concept were then selected via manual inspection of random response samples across all 21 configurations. Like the trait flags, concept-category flags are binary per response and case-insensitive.

Lastly, for agent individuation, every reasoning passage is embedded with OpenAI’s text-embedding-3-large model oai_embeddings (3,072 dimensions). Two questions follow, both restricted to agents whose 5 repetitions all returned the same decision (the “5/5 agreement” subset). The first: how similar are an agent’s own 5 responses? Within-agent similarity is computed as the mean of the 10 pairwise cosines among an agent’s 5 reps, then averaged across all qualifying 5/5 agreements for each LLM. The second: how similar are two different agents who reach the same decision at the same infection level? Across-agent similarity is computed bucket by bucket (one bucket per infection level and decision direction): every pair of qualifying agents in the bucket contributes the mean of the 25 rep-pair cosines between them, and the result is averaged across all such pairs.

4.4.1 Mobility Curve Results

The 21 mobility curves vary dramatically: average stay-home rates span nearly the full 0–100% range across the 21 configurations. The six subsections below examine variation across LLMs from six different angles. §4.4.1.1 examines the role of the LLM provider; §4.4.1.2 the role of reasoning level; §4.4.1.3 the role of model size within a provider family; §4.4.1.4 the role of model evolution within a family across time; §4.4.1.5 the role of knowledge cutoff; and §4.4.1.6 the role of release date.

4.4.1.1 Cross-provider differences (RQ1) — Does LLM provider affect mobility curves?

The mobility-curve landscape within each provider is itself wide. Figure 10 overlays all configurations from each of the three providers, showing the spread of mobility responses across the configurations included in this study. Within OpenAI alone, mobility ranges from near-100% stay-home (GPT-3.5 Turbo, GPT-5.2 off) to near-0% stay-home (GPT-4o); within Anthropic, from Claude 3 Haiku at the cautious end to Claude Haiku 4.5 at the mobile end; within Google, the spread is narrower but still spans the lower half of the mobility range. There is no single “Anthropic look” or “OpenAI look” to a mobility curve once different models within each provider are considered.

To make a controlled cross-provider comparison, we hold model size (flagship within each provider, as of the December 2025 data-collection window) and reasoning level (off) constant. Figure 11 shows the resulting flagship mobility curves: GPT-5.2 (OpenAI), Claude Opus 4.5 (Anthropic), and Gemini 3 Flash Preview (Google). All three start near 100% mobility at 0% infection, but the curves diverge sharply as soon as any community infection is reported. GPT-5.2 becomes cautious almost immediately — its mobility drops to near zero by 0.3% infection and stays there across the rest of the range. Claude Opus 4.5 and Gemini 3 Flash Preview, in contrast, behave as relatively mobile agents across the full range; their mobility hovers in the 75–90% band and declines only gradually as infection rises. The multilinear regression confirms this pattern: GPT-5.2 differs significantly from the other two flagships, while Claude Opus 4.5 and Gemini 3 Flash Preview are not statistically distinguishable from each other (Appendix D).

The two figures together suggest that the choice of LLM matters along multiple dimensions. Within each provider, model variants span a wide mobility range; across providers, even matched flagships occupy distinct regions of the mobility space. For a researcher building a generative-agent simulation, neither “pick any provider” nor “pick the flagship” produces interchangeable behavior.

4.4.1.2 Reasoning level (RQ2) — Does LLM reasoning level affect mobility curves?

Reasoning level is a configurable LLM parameter controlling how much hidden deliberation the model performs before producing its final response. Two configurations span the full reasoning-level ladder (off, low, medium, high): GPT-5.2 (OpenAI) and Gemini 3 Flash Preview (Google). Figure 12 places these two reasoning ladders side by side, with each panel showing how the same model’s mobility curve responds to changes in reasoning level.

For GPT-5.2, increasing reasoning shifts the mobility curve modestly upward — mobility rises by roughly 9 percentage points from reasoning off to reasoning high. For Gemini 3 Flash Preview, the direction is opposite: enabling any non-zero reasoning level shifts the curve downward by roughly 9 percentage points relative to reasoning off, with little additional change between low, medium, and high. The multilinear regression in Appendix D reports which configurations differ significantly.

Reasoning is a real but minor lever, and its sign differs between the two LLMs tested. Both shifts are roughly 9 percentage points and small compared to the cross-provider gap reported in §4.4.1.1. The result complicates any general claim that more reasoning makes a generative agent uniformly more or less cautious.

4.4.1.3 Model size (RQ3) — Does LLM size affect mobility curves?

Model size refers to a provider’s relative scale tier within a single family of releases. Two providers in the data have multiple model sizes within a single generation: Anthropic’s Claude 4.5 family (Haiku [small], Sonnet [middle], Opus [large]) and Google’s Gemini 2.5 family (Flash Lite [small], Flash [middle]). All five configurations are evaluated with reasoning off. Figure 13 places the two size ladders side by side.

For the Anthropic Claude 4.5 family, mobility does not change monotonically with size. Going from Haiku (small) to Sonnet (middle) shifts the curve substantially downward — Sonnet sits well below Haiku across the full infection range. But going from Sonnet to Opus (large) reverses direction: Opus tracks close to Haiku rather than continuing further down. For the Google Gemini 2.5 family, Gemini 2.5 Flash sits well above Gemini 2.5 Flash Lite across most of the infection range. Flash Lite itself shows an unusual within-curve pattern — its mobility starts moderate at 0% infection, dips sharply between 0.1% and 0.9%, jumps back at 1.0%, and stabilizes above 50% from 1.2% onwards. One possible explanation is that Flash Lite, a smaller and lower-capacity variant of Gemini 2.5 Flash, is oversensitive to sub-1% infection levels; it reacts more strongly to fractional values in the 0.1–0.9% range than to values at or above 1%. Other small models in this study (Claude 3 Haiku, Claude Haiku 4.5) do not show this dip-and-recover pattern, so size alone does not fully account for the behavior. Across both providers’ size ladders, the data shows substantial variation but no consistent direction of effect attributable to size. The multilinear regression in Appendix D reports which configurations differ significantly.

Model evolution refers to the sequence of major releases within a single provider’s family over time, where each successive release typically incorporates new training data, refined alignment, and architectural improvements. All three providers contribute model evolution data: Anthropic from Claude Sonnet 4.0 to Claude Sonnet 4.5; OpenAI from GPT-3.5 Turbo through GPT-4o, GPT-4.1, GPT-5.1, and GPT-5.2; and Google from Gemini 2.0 Flash through Gemini 2.5 Flash to Gemini 3 Flash Preview. 4.4.1.5 Knowledge cutoff (RQ5) — Does LLM knowledge cutoff affect mobility curves?

The 21 configurations span knowledge cutoffs from September 2021 (GPT-3.5 Turbo) to August 2025 (Claude Opus 4.5 and GPT-5.2). 4.4.2 Persona Results

The four subsections below examine the agent-level predictors of the stay-home decision and how the 21 LLMs vary in their treatment of those predictors. §4.4.2.1 examines the direction of each persona predictor’s effect (RQ7); §4.4.2.2 ranks persona predictors by their relative importance within and across LLMs (RQ8); §4.4.2.3 the magnitude of all persona effects combined relative to the infection-level effect (RQ9); and §4.4.2.4 agreement across LLMs on which agent personas are most cautious (RQ10).

4.4.2.1 Trait effect directions (RQ7) — What is the direction of each persona predictor’s effect on the stay-home decision?

Agent traits enter the random-effects logit (§4.3.7.2) as predictors: five Big Five trait dummies, a male dummy, and age in years. Each Big Five dummy equals 1 for the named pole (extraverted, agreeable, conscientious, emotionally stable, open to experience) and 0 for its opposite (introverted, antagonistic, unconscientious, neurotic, closed to experience). The male dummy is 1 for male and 0 for female. Age is a continuous predictor; its coefficient is per year, and is scaled by 47 (the 18–65 span) for magnitude comparisons against the binary traits. Each predictor’s β gives the log-odds shift in stay-home probability associated with the dummy = 1 value, holding the other predictors fixed. A negative β on a trait dummy means agents at that pole are more mobile, and a positive β means they stay home more.

Figure 19 shows the per-predictor β coefficients across all 21 LLM configurations, one panel per predictor, with significance markers and 95% confidence intervals. The right column of each panel reports a normalized |β| / infection-range ratio, where infection range is the maximum log-odds shift the infection signal produces over the 0–7% range.

For four of the five Big Five dimensions, every LLM agrees on the sign of the coefficient. Extraversion is negative in all 21 configurations (significant in 20). Agreeableness is positive in all 21 (significant in 18). Conscientiousness is positive in all 21 (significant in 19). Emotional stability is negative in 20 of 21 (significant in 19). The unanimity holds within each provider: all 5 Anthropic, all 9 OpenAI, and all 7 Gemini configurations sign these four traits in the same direction.

Openness to experience is the least consistent Big Five dimension: 14 of 21 configurations show a significant positive effect, and only one (Gemini 2.5 Flash Lite) shows a significant negative effect. Gender (male) is significant in only 3 of 21 configurations and varies in direction across configurations. Age (scaled across the 18–65 range) is significant in 6 of 21 configurations, mostly within OpenAI.

4.4.2.2 Predictor importance (RQ8) — Which persona predictors most strongly drive the stay-home decision?

To rank persona predictors by influence within and across LLMs, each predictor’s |β| is divided by that LLM’s infection range (§4.4.2.1). A ratio above 1× means the predictor alone shifts the decision more than infection’s full effect. Figure 20 plots each configuration’s predictor ratios on one row, with a vertical 1× line marking the threshold.

Gemini configurations sit furthest right. Four of the five Big Five traits have median ratios above 1× (extraversion 1.83, conscientiousness 1.40, emotional stability 1.35, openness 1.09; only agreeableness sits just below at 0.93), meaning each of those four traits typically exceeds infection’s full effect on its own. Anthropic configurations cluster lower, with median ratios spanning a wide range (conscientiousness 0.75, extraversion 0.64, emotional stability 0.61, agreeableness 0.27, openness 0.04); across all five configurations and five Big Five traits, only one ratio crosses 1× (Claude Opus 4.5’s conscientiousness, at 1.09×). OpenAI configurations cluster lowest, with all five Big Five median ratios below 0.4× (extraversion 0.35, emotional stability 0.24, conscientiousness 0.19, agreeableness 0.17, openness 0.15); no OpenAI trait coefficient anywhere in the figure exceeds 1×, indicating that for OpenAI configurations trait identity is a smaller share of the decision than that of infection level. Across all three providers, gender and age (diamonds) sit near zero on the ratio axis, consistent with their low significance counts in §4.4.2.1.

The rank-1 predictor (the predictor with the largest |β| / infection ratio) within each configuration also splits along provider lines. In all 9 OpenAI configurations, infection level is the rank-1 predictor. In 4 of 5 Anthropic configurations, infection is rank-1; only Claude Opus 4.5 promotes a Big Five trait (conscientiousness) to rank-1. In 6 of 7 Gemini configurations a Big Five trait is rank-1 (extraversion in 4 configurations, emotional stability in 2); only Gemini 2.0 Flash keeps infection at rank-1. The disagreement on which predictor matters most is itself a provider-level pattern: OpenAI is uniformly infection-dominated, Gemini is uniformly trait-dominated, and Anthropic is mostly infection-dominated with a single Opus exception.

4.4.2.3 Persona vs. infection magnitude (RQ9) — Are personality effects larger than infection effects?

The persona-to-infection ratio compares total persona influence against the infection effect. The denominator is the same infection range used in §4.4.2.1 and §4.4.2.2; the numerator, the personality range, is the maximum gap in stay-home log-odds achievable by varying an agent’s persona (Big Five traits, gender, age) at zero infection — a sum of |β| across all persona predictors from the random-effects logit. A ratio above 1× means persona variation alone shifts the decision more than infection’s full effect. Figure 21 plots both ranges on a common log-odds scale: the solid line spans the personality range at zero infection, and the dashed line shifts the same range by the maximum infection contribution.

Across the 21 configurations, 19 have a persona-to-infection ratio above 1× and the median ratio is 2.75×. The provider-level pattern parallels the per-trait pattern in §4.4.2.1. Gemini configurations are the most persona-dominated, with a median ratio of 7.96×: five of the seven Gemini configurations (Gemini 2.5 Flash plus all four Gemini 3 Flash Preview reasoning levels) reach ratios between 7.07× and 8.85×, while the older Gemini 2.0 Flash and Gemini 2.5 Flash Lite sit lower at 2.93× and 2.75×. Anthropic configurations are intermediate, with a median of 2.84× and a spread from 1.17× (Claude 3 Haiku) to 3.67× (Claude Opus 4.5). OpenAI configurations are the most infection-dominated, with a median of 1.37× and most configurations between 1× and 2×; GPT-4.1 (3.09×) sits noticeably above the OpenAI median, and two configurations sit below 1× (GPT-5.2 off at 0.08×, GPT-5.2 low at 0.81×).

Beyond the persona-to-infection ratio, Figure 21 also shows where each LLM’s personality range sits on the absolute log-odds scale at 0% infection. For 19 of the 21 configurations, the personality range crosses the 50% P(stay-home) threshold: the most-cautious persona sits above 50% and the most-mobile persona sits below, with infection then shifting the range further toward stay-home. Two configurations are exceptions, both with personality ranges entirely below 50%: Claude Haiku 4.5 and GPT-4o. For these two LLMs, every persona is inclined to go out at 0% infection regardless of trait combination. No configuration has a personality range sitting entirely above 50% — at zero infection, every LLM produces at least one persona that would go out.

4.4.2.4 Agent identity agreement (RQ10) — Do LLMs agree on which agent personas are most cautious?

For each LLM configuration, the 100 agents are independently ranked by their stay-home rate within that configuration — the fraction of the agent’s 200 decisions (40 infection levels × 5 reps) on which the agent chose to stay home. Rank 1 is the most cautious persona under that LLM. Ties are resolved by average-rank tie handling. Each agent then has 21 ranks, one for each LLM configuration. Figure 22 summarizes each agent’s 21 ranks: the dot is the median rank the agent received, the dark bar spans the IQR (25th–75th percentile), and the pale whisker spans the full range. Agents are sorted top-to-bottom by median rank.

Agreement among LLMs is uneven across rank positions. The median IQR among the top-25 most-cautious agents is 16 ranks; among the bottom-25 most-mobile agents, 14 ranks; but among the middle 50 agents it widens to 26 ranks. LLMs converge more readily on agents at the extremes than on agents in the middle of the cautious-to-mobile distribution. The median full range echoes this pattern — 48 ranks among the top 25, 62 among the bottom 25, and 68 among the middle 50. No agent escapes substantial outlier disagreement: the smallest range across the 21 LLMs is 27 ranks, and the median agent has a 66-rank range.

4.4.3 Response Results

The four subsections below examine the content of the reasoning passage. §4.4.3.1 (RQ11) reports how often each LLM mentions Big Five trait, infection, and age concepts; §4.4.3.2 (RQ12) tests whether, conditional on a mention, the mention amplifies or contradicts the trait’s behavioral effect; §4.4.3.3 (RQ13) examines which concept categories are cited in stay-home versus go-out reasoning; and §4.4.3.4 (RQ14) measures how agent-specific each LLM’s reasoning text is.

4.4.3.1 Mention rates (RQ11) — How often do LLMs mention trait, infection, and age concepts in their reasoning?

A keyword dictionary (§4.3.7.3) tags each response for mentions of the five Big Five trait labels, the agent’s infection level, and the agent’s age. A trait counts as mentioned whenever a keyword from either pole appears (e.g., either “introverted” or “extraverted” registers as an extraversion mention). Figure 23 reports the resulting per-LLM mention rates, broken out by the agent’s assigned Big Five pole. Cells outside the [15%, 85%] gate are dimmed: at those rates the corresponding mention flag carries no usable variation and is excluded from the mention-flag logit used in §4.4.3.2.

Mention rates vary widely both within and across providers (Figure 23). Among the Big Five labels, conscientiousness is mentioned most often (mean 55.8% across the 21 configurations) and openness least (mean 16.6%). Provider-level differences are substantial: average Big Five mention rate is 57.7% across the five Anthropic configurations, 17.1% across the nine OpenAI configurations, and 48.7% across the seven Gemini configurations. Infection level is mentioned in nearly every response on most LLMs (mean rate 87.8%) and falls outside the 15–85% gate for most of them, so the infection mention flag enters the §4.4.3.2 regression for only 4 of 21 configurations. Age is rarely cited anywhere (mean 4.0% across configurations). GPT-3.5 Turbo is an outlier on every dimension, mentioning infection in only 0.2% of responses and Big Five labels in under 2%.

4.4.3.2 Mention amplification (RQ12) — When a trait is mentioned, does the mention amplify or contradict the trait’s behavioral effect?

The mention-flag logit (§4.3.7.3) extends the random-effects spec from §4.4.2 with one binary flag per Big Five trait label, plus one for infection and one for age. A flag enters the model only when its overall mention rate from Figure 23 falls in [15%, 85%], for a total of twelve mention coefficients reported below: ten Big Five pole flags plus the infection and age flags. Each mention coefficient is the log-odds shift in stay-home probability when its concept is mentioned. A positive coefficient indicates the mention pushes the decision toward staying home and a negative coefficient toward going out. Figure 24 plots those coefficients in absolute log-odds.

For Extraversion, mentioning “introverted” shifts behavior toward stay-home in 9 of 12 configurations (1 go-out, 2 not significant); mentioning “extraverted” shifts toward go-out in 9 of 11 (2 not significant). Introverted agents who mention their trait stay home even more; extraverted agents who mention theirs go out even more.

For Agreeableness, mentioning “antagonistic” shifts toward go-out in 10 of 11 configurations (1 stay-home); mentioning “agreeable” is mixed — 5 stay-home, 4 go-out, 2 not significant out of 11. Antagonistic agents who mention their trait go out even more; agreeable agents who mention theirs split roughly evenly on direction.

For Conscientiousness, mentioning “unconscientious” shifts toward go-out in 8 of 13 configurations (2 stay-home, 3 not significant); mentioning “conscientious” shifts toward go-out in 9 of 11 (2 not significant). Unconscientious agents who mention their trait go out even more, following the trait coefficient. Conscientious agents who mention theirs also shift toward going out — the lone paradox among the Big Five, opposite to the trait coefficient.

For Neuroticism, mentioning “neurotic” shifts toward stay-home in all 12 configurations; mentioning “emotionally stable” shifts toward go-out in all 12. Neurotic agents who mention their trait stay home even more; emotionally stable agents who mention theirs go out even more.

For Openness, mentioning “closed” shifts toward go-out in all 7 estimable configurations; mentioning “open” is split 3 stay-home, 3 go-out, 4 not significant out of 10. Closed agents who mention their trait go out even more; open agents who mention theirs are mixed across LLMs.

The infection flag is estimable in only 4 of 21 configurations; the rest fall outside the 15–85% gate because nearly every response mentions infection. In all 4 it is significantly stay-home: explicit mention of infection makes the stay-home decision more likely beyond what the infection level alone predicts. The age flag is estimable in only one configuration (Gemini 2.5 Flash), where it points toward going out; with a single estimable LLM the pattern is too thin to generalize.

4.4.3.3 Decision themes (RQ13) — Which concept categories are cited in stay-home vs. go-out reasoning?

Sections 4.4.3.1 and 4.4.3.2 describe how often each concept is mentioned and whether mentioning a trait amplifies its behavioral effect. This subsection asks a complementary question: are concepts cited more often in stay-home or in go-out reasoning? Figure 25 reports this for the ten Big Five trait labels plus infection and age. Each cell is the mention rate among that subgroup’s stay-home responses minus the mention rate among its go-out responses, in percentage points (pp). For example, Claude Opus 4.5’s Introverted column reads +16 pp: 78% of Opus 4.5’s introverted agents mention an extraversion keyword when staying home, versus 62% when going out.

Per §4.4.2.1, agents at the cautious-coded poles (introverted, agreeable, conscientious, neurotic, and open to experience) tend to stay home more, while their mobile counterparts (extraverted, antagonistic, unconscientious, emotionally stable, and closed to experience) go out more. Figure 25’s median row mostly aligns with these trait directions. Among the cautious-coded poles: introverted is cited a median 16 pp more often in stay-home responses, agreeable and conscientious show no skew (both 0 pp), and neurotic is cited 20 pp more in stay-home; open to experience is the surprising exception at −2 pp, slightly leaning go-out, matching the mixed pattern §4.4.3.2 flagged for the open pole. Among the mobile-coded poles: extraverted is cited 10 pp more in go-out responses, antagonistic 9 pp more, unconscientious 10 pp more, emotionally stable 13 pp more, and closed to experience 8 pp more. Infection and age each lean slightly toward stay-home (+2 pp and +1 pp).

Figure 26 extends Figure 25’s Δ comparison to a broader keyword set (§4.3.7.3) covering Work, Community, Self Health, Virus Properties, and Virus Prevalence — categories chosen because each one is directly cued by the prompt template (Figure 9).

Concept categories show much sharper stay-vs-go asymmetries than trait labels. For stay-home reasoning, Community is cited a median 44 pp more often than in go-out, followed by Virus Properties at +20 pp and Virus Prevalence at +9 pp. On the go-out side, Self Health is cited a median 37 pp more often in go-out reasoning, with Work obligations 7 pp behind. Stay-home reasoning skews toward other-focused concepts (Community, Virus Properties); go-out reasoning skews toward self-focused concepts (Self Health, Work).

4.4.3.4 Agent individuation (RQ14) — How agent-specific is each LLM’s reasoning text?

Sections 4.4.3.1–4.4.3.3 measure which specific concepts agents cite and how those mentions correlate with each decision. This subsection asks a higher-level question: how much does the reasoning text itself vary across agents? The agent-individuation analysis (§4.3.7.3) embeds every reasoning passage and, restricted to the “5/5 agreement” subset, computes two cosine summaries per LLM: within-agent similarity (the mean cosine across an agent’s 5 reps) and across-agent similarity (the mean cosine between agents who reached the same decision at the same infection level). The gap Δ = (within − across) measures agent individuation: a large positive Δ means the LLM produces text more similar within an agent than across agents (agent-specific reasoning); a near-zero Δ means similar text across different agents (templated text).

Cosine similarity values can be hard to interpret in isolation. Figure 27 anchors the scale with three example response pairs from Claude Opus 4.5 at infection 3.5%, all at the same go-out decision: a near-paraphrase pair from one agent’s two reps (≈ 0.99), a mid-similarity pair from two different agents (≈ 0.85), and a low-similarity pair from two different agents whose go-out reasoning diverges (≈ 0.65).

Figure 28 reports the within−across gap (Δ) per LLM. Each row shows the within-agent (filled) and across-agent (hollow) cosine values connected by a line; rows are grouped by provider, and marker color encodes the LLM’s sampling temperature (blue = 0, orange = 1).

All 21 LLMs have a positive Δ, meaning every LLM produces at least somewhat agent-specific reasoning, though magnitudes vary considerably. Gemini configurations show the largest gap (median Δ = 0.13; range 0.11 to 0.24), Anthropic is intermediate (median 0.12; range 0.08 to 0.16), and OpenAI sits lowest (median 0.04; range 0.01 to 0.16). Within OpenAI, reasoning-enabled configurations produce notably more templated text than reasoning-off ones: the GPT-5.2 ladder yields Δ between 0.01 and 0.03, o3 sits at 0.04, while GPT-4o (Δ = 0.16) and GPT-4.1 (Δ = 0.15) reach the upper end of the OpenAI range. The provider ordering (Gemini » Anthropic » OpenAI) parallels the persona-effect-magnitude ordering from §4.4.2.2 and §4.4.2.3: LLMs that weight persona inputs more heavily in their decisions also produce more agent-specific reasoning text.

4.5 Discussion

The Discussion takes up the three results subsections in turn: §4.5.1 the mobility-curve findings (RQ1–RQ6), §4.5.2 the persona-effect findings (RQ7–RQ10), and §4.5.3 the reasoning-text findings (RQ11–RQ14). §4.5.4 distills the cross-cutting implications for GABM practitioners, and §4.5.5 records the study’s limitations.

4.5.1 Mobility Curve Discussion (RQ1–RQ6)

The 21 mobility curves span nearly the full 0–100% stay-home range, but the practical reading for a researcher building a generative-agent simulation is narrow: neither “pick any provider” nor “pick the flagship” produces interchangeable behavior (RQ1). At the matched flagships-at-reasoning-off comparison (Figure 11), GPT-5.2 collapses to near-zero mobility almost immediately, while Claude Opus 4.5 and Gemini 3 Flash Preview remain in the 75–90% mobility band across the infection range and are statistically indistinguishable from each other (Appendix D). Within each provider, model variants themselves span a wide mobility range comparable to the across-provider gap (Figure 10).

Of the five within-family lenses, only reasoning level (RQ2) produces a measurable shift, and even that effect is small and direction-ambiguous. Reasoning level moves mobility by roughly 9 percentage points (Figure 12), with opposite signs in the two configurations sampled across the full ladder: increasing reasoning makes GPT-5.2 more mobile and Gemini 3 Flash Preview less mobile. A practitioner can expect reasoning level to nudge generative-agent behavior, but cannot predict the direction of that nudge without testing the specific model.

The remaining four lenses do not generate actionable insight. Model size within a family (RQ3), model evolution within a provider (RQ4), knowledge cutoff (RQ5), and release date (RQ6) all fail to predict mobility-curve placement (Figures 13–18). Configurations that share any of these properties still land at very different positions on the mobility axis. Same-family models at different sizes, successive releases within a family, models with knowledge cutoffs in the same three-month window, and models released within a four-month window each span much of the 0–100% range. A researcher cannot predict an LLM’s mobility curve from its size, generation, knowledge cutoff, or release date. Identifying what within an LLM’s training pipeline produces a particular mobility tendency — alignment objectives, the RLHF system, system-prompt conventions, etc. — is left as future work.

4.5.2 Persona Discussion (RQ7–RQ10)

The persona analysis shows two complementary patterns: directions agree across LLMs, magnitudes do not. For four of the five Big Five traits, every LLM agrees on the direction of the effect (RQ7, Figure 19). 21 of 21 agree that extraverted agents are more mobile and that agreeable and conscientious agents stay home more, and 20 of 21 agree that emotionally stable agents are more mobile. Openness is the lone Big Five inconsistency, mostly positive (14 of 21 significantly positive, 1 significantly negative). Gender and age rarely reach significance. The unanimity holds within each provider as well as across providers. A plausible reading is that LLMs have inherited a shared mapping from Big Five labels to risk-related behavior, learned from broadly similar training corpora. The direction-agreement parallels Mooney et al.’s finding mooney2025 that agents’ surface-level responses align with stated profiles across nine models from three model families. This is reassuring for GABM epidemic model realism: swapping the LLM does not typically change which traits make a generative agent more cautious.

The importance of each predictor (RQ8, Figure 20), in contrast, splits sharply along provider lines. In OpenAI, infection level is the top predictor in all 9 configurations, and no Big Five trait coefficient anywhere exceeds the infection coefficient. In Anthropic, infection is the top predictor in 4 of 5; Claude Opus 4.5 is the exception (conscientiousness). In Gemini, a Big Five trait is the top predictor in 6 of 7 configurations (extraversion in four, emotional stability in two). In fact, four of the five Gemini Big Five median trait coefficients exceed infection (extraversion, conscientiousness, emotional stability, and openness).

For most LLMs, the combined persona effect, summed across all persona predictors at once (Big Five, gender, age), outweighs the community-infection signal in driving the stay-home decision (RQ9, Figure 21). 19 of 21 configurations show a personality range (the total log-odds gap from varying all persona predictors) exceeding the infection range, with a median ratio of 2.75×. The pattern is not universal: the two GPT-5.2 settings at off and low reasoning are infection-dominated. These are also the configurations where mobility collapses to near zero across the full infection range (Figure 12), suggesting that when an LLM is maximally cautious to the infection signal it crowds the persona signal out. The provider-level spread (Gemini median 7.96×, Anthropic 2.84×, OpenAI 1.37×) means the relative weight of persona versus environment in driving agent decisions differs roughly six-fold across providers. A simulation built on a Gemini backbone will exhibit more persona-driven heterogeneity in agent behavior than the same simulation built on an OpenAI backbone, even with identical agent population, prompt, and infection signal.

Agreement among LLMs on agent identity (RQ10, Figure 22) is uneven across the cautious-to-mobile distribution. At the extremes, LLMs agree more closely: the median IQR among the 25 most-cautious agents is 16 ranks, and among the 25 most-mobile is 14 ranks; in the middle the IQR widens to 26 ranks. But the agreement is only relative. No agent escapes substantial outlier disagreement: the most-agreed-upon agent has a 27-rank gap between its highest and lowest LLM ranks, and the median agent has a 66-rank range. This pattern is consistent with the trait-direction finding above. Agents whose profiles stack many cautious-coded traits (or many mobile-coded traits) get ranked similarly on every LLM because the direction of each trait’s effect is universal. Agents with mixed trait profiles are sensitive to which traits a given LLM weighs more heavily, and that weighting is itself a provider-level pattern.

4.5.3 Response Discussion (RQ11–RQ14)

LLMs differ sharply in how often they cite trait, infection, and age concepts in their reasoning (RQ11, Figure 23). Average Big Five mention rate is 57.7% across the five Anthropic configurations, 48.7% across the seven Gemini configurations, and 17.1% across the nine OpenAI configurations. Most LLMs cite infection in nearly every response (mean 87.8%), age rarely (mean 4.0%), and GPT-3.5 Turbo is an outlier on every dimension (under 2% on Big Five labels, 0.2% on infection). The citation ordering Anthropic » Gemini » OpenAI does not match the persona-effect-magnitude ordering Gemini » Anthropic » OpenAI from §4.5.2. Gemini weighs trait identity most heavily in its decisions but surfaces trait language less often in its text than Anthropic does. With that being said, mentioning a trait and weighting a trait are not the same lever: a model can write less about persona while still letting persona drive the decision more.

When a trait is mentioned, the mention usually amplifies the trait’s behavioral direction (RQ12, Figure 24). For 8 of the 10 Big Five trait labels, the significant mention shifts align with the trait’s direction in the persona regression: introverted agents who name their trait stay home even more, extraverted agents who name theirs go out even more, etc. Openness and conscientiousness are the two exceptions. For openness, the “open” mention is genuinely split: 3 configurations shift toward staying home, 3 toward going out, and 4 produce no significant shift out of 10 estimable. “Open to experience” can support either decision in pandemic reasoning: an agent might write “I’m open to learning more about this, so let me stay home and follow guidance” or “I’m open to new experiences, so let me go out.” Conscientiousness produces the lone systematic paradox: mentioning “conscientious” shifts the log-odds toward going out in 9 of 11 estimable configurations, opposite the direction of the conscientious-stay-home coefficient. A plausible reading is that conscientiousness gets invoked instrumentally rather than as a stay-home marker. For example, Claude Opus 4.5’s agent Jaime, while going out, writes “I’m conscientious about my work responsibilities and need to go to the office” (Figure 27).

Concepts cited in stay-home reasoning differ systematically from those cited in go-out reasoning (RQ13). For trait labels (Figure 25), the median pattern roughly tracks each trait’s direction in the persona regression: cautious-coded labels (introverted, neurotic) are cited more often in stay-home responses, and mobile-coded labels (extraverted, antagonistic, unconscientious, emotionally stable, closed) more often in go-out responses. Open to experience is the exception, leaning slightly go-out (−2 pp) despite being cautious-coded. This matches the mixed open-mention pattern from RQ12 and openness’s role as the most inconsistent Big Five direction in §4.5.2 (RQ7).

Beyond trait labels, broader concept categories show much sharper asymmetries (Figure 26). Community is cited a median 44 pp more often in stay-home reasoning than in go-out, and Virus Properties (+20 pp) and Virus Prevalence (+9 pp) reinforce the stay-home skew. Self Health is cited 37 pp more often in go-out reasoning, with Work obligations 7 pp behind. Stay-home reasoning skews toward other-focused concepts (Community, Virus Properties); go-out reasoning skews toward self-focused concepts (Self Health, Work).

All 21 LLMs produce at least somewhat agent-specific reasoning text (RQ14, Figure 28): every configuration shows a positive within−across cosine gap (Δ). Magnitudes vary in a familiar provider-level pattern. Gemini configurations show the largest gap (median Δ = 0.13; range 0.11 to 0.24), Anthropic is intermediate (median 0.12; range 0.08 to 0.16), and OpenAI sits lowest (median 0.04; range 0.01 to 0.16). The provider ordering Gemini » Anthropic » OpenAI parallels the persona-effect-magnitude ordering from §4.5.2: LLMs that weight persona inputs more heavily in their decisions also produce more agent-specific reasoning text.

Within OpenAI, reasoning-enabled configurations produce notably more templated text than reasoning-off ones: the GPT-5.2 ladder yields Δ between 0.01 and 0.03, o3 sits at 0.04, while reasoning-off GPT-4o (0.16) and GPT-4.1 (0.15) reach the upper end of the OpenAI range. This tracks the persona-vs-infection finding from §4.5.2 (RQ9): the reasoning-on GPT-5.2 configurations are also the most infection-dominated in OpenAI.

4.5.4 Implications for GABM design

GABM was introduced to replace explicit behavioral parameterization ghaffarzadegan2024. Agents derive their decisions from a well-trained LLM instead of from modeler-coded decision rules. The variation across LLMs documented here gives that move a sharper edge: the LLM choice itself becomes the parameterization. This empirically reinforces recent concerns about generative-ABM validation larooij_tornberg2026,liwu2025 and aligns with a growing body of shallow behavioral probes showing that LLM choice measurably moves agent output dominguez_olmedo2024,einwiller2025,mooney2025,parikh2026. Four practical implications follow for researchers building generative-agent simulations.

The choice of LLM is not interchangeable. Aggregate mobility, the magnitude of persona effects, the relative weight of persona versus infection signal, and the agent-specificity of reasoning text all depend on the LLM.

No pattern within a provider’s lineup predicts agent behavior. The lenses practitioners typically use to choose between models — bigger model, newer release, more recent training data, more reasoning — do not, in this domain, predict generative-agent behavior. A researcher who upgrades from one model to its successor, scales to a larger model in the same family, or enables reasoning should not assume agent behavior will change in a known direction.

Persona inputs generalize directionally but not in magnitude. Assigning Big Five trait labels to agents will reliably push behavior in expected directions on every LLM tested. But the magnitude of those effects varies several-fold across providers and is not predictable in advance for any specific LLM.

The reasoning text is real signal, not post-hoc justification. When an agent mentions a trait or invokes a particular decision theme (§4.5.3), those textual choices correlate with the decision the agent makes. For GABM researchers, this argues for retaining and analyzing the reasoning text rather than discarding it.

4.5.5 Limitations

Several limitations of this study point to natural extensions. The LLM sample, while spanning three major providers and 21 distinct configurations, is small relative to the broader population of available models. Additionally, the rapidly-evolving LLM landscape means today’s findings are tied to a particular snapshot of each provider’s training pipeline.

The probe is also monetarily intensive. The 420,000 decision queries plus the embedding step for §4.4.3.4 generated tens of thousands of API calls per LLM and amounted to roughly $840 in API spend. As LLM costs continue to fall and local-running models become more capable, the cost barrier to probe studies of this scale should ease, but for now it remains a meaningful constraint.

The probe also restricts to a single binary decision (stay home or go out) on a single 100-agent population. Whether the provider-level patterns documented here generalize to other decision domains or to larger and differently-distributed agent populations is an open question.

The keyword dictionaries used for §4.4.3.1–§4.4.3.3 (defined in §4.3.7.3) were curated by manual sample inspection rather than learned from the data. While this matches conventions in content-analysis research, an unsupervised approach (for example, topic modeling on the response embeddings) might surface decision themes the manual approach misses.

Lastly, the probe evaluates general-purpose chat models with light persona prompts. Whether richer interview-style prompting park2024_1000, fine-tuning on human-behavior traces kolluri2025, or purpose-built behavioral models simile_ai would reduce the model-to-model variability documented here is left as future work.

4.6 Conclusion

This study probed 21 LLM configurations from three providers across 100 frozen personas, 40 infection levels, and 5 repetitions per cell, totaling 420,000 binary decisions, to ask how the choice of LLM shapes generative-agent behavior. Mobility-curve, persona, and response findings all varied across LLMs. The model attributes a researcher would typically use to choose an LLM (size, generation, knowledge cutoff, release date) did not predict that variation. Across LLMs the direction of trait effects agreed; magnitude varied several-fold by provider. The reasoning text turned out to encode predictive signal beyond the decision itself, revealing an other-focused-vs-self-focused framing of the stay-home choice.

GABM was introduced to replace rule-based decision-making with LLM-mediated decisions ghaffarzadegan2024. Human behavior resists clean mathematical specification: how the death of a grandparent during an epidemic alters an agent’s mobility, for instance, is hard to derive from a rule. By connecting agents to a well-trained LLM, the modeler offloads that specification problem. The variation across LLMs observed here means that move has a hidden cost: the LLM choice itself becomes the parameterization. The implication is not that practitioners should pick an LLM that produces a desired behavioral profile; that would simply re-introduce the parameter-tuning subjectivity GABM was meant to escape. The implication is that GABM, as currently constituted with general-purpose LLMs, is most defensible for qualitative claims (which agree across LLMs) and weakest for quantitative claims (which vary several-fold by provider).

Purpose-built models are emerging in response. Recent work fine-tunes general-purpose LLMs on real human-action traces to improve behavioral-simulation accuracy kolluri2025, and well-funded efforts like Simile.ai simile_ai are building LLMs specifically to capture the population-level diversity of human decision-making rather than the single, polished persona of a chat assistant. Whether magnitude reliability emerges as general-purpose models scale, or whether it requires this kind of specialized training, is an open question this study cannot answer. Until then, GABM practitioners should treat the LLM choice as the central parameter in their model and report LLM sensitivity alongside any quantitative claim.

Modelers from epidemiology, sociology, political science, economics, and complex-systems modeling could extend the probe approach to their own decision domains, and LLM-sensitivity testing could be treated as a routine part of any GABM publication’s evidentiary base, much as classical agent-based modeling has long been expected to demonstrate sensitivity to parameter choices.

Chapter 5: Conclusion

This dissertation set out to address a methodological gap in epidemic modeling: how to incorporate human behavior into infectious-disease simulations when behavior resists clean mathematical specification. The stakes of getting this right became vivid during the COVID-19 pandemic, which killed millions worldwide who_covid_deaths; in the United States, the recorded death toll surpassed 100,000 within roughly four months of the first confirmed case and reached one million by 2022 cdc_covid_deaths. Human behavior shaped that loss of life at every turn. Whether people distanced, masked, and isolated, and whether governments imposed or relaxed restrictions, set the timing and height of each epidemic wave.

The forecasting models, meant to guide the response, failed to represent how human behavior would change case counts, and the resulting forecasts swung wildly — misdirecting scarce resources and mistiming interventions at the moments that mattered most rahmandad2022_forecasting,rahmandad2022_missing. New York planned for as many as 140,000 hospital beds at the peak, more than seven times the roughly 18,500 patients actually hospitalized when the wave crested feuer2020. Then-Governor Andrew Cuomo attributed the gap to the behavior of New Yorkers, who had stayed home and kept their distance feuer2020 weeks later, he said flatly of the projection models, “They were all wrong” ioannidis2022. Likewise, a separate simulator covid19sim projected more than 23,000 deaths in the month after Georgia reopened against 896 observed, as residents stayed cautious even after restrictions lifted ioannidis2022. In each case the forecasts erred because they treated the level of social contact as an exogenous scenario input rather than letting it respond to rising risk, missing the feedback by which people and governments tighten and relax precautions as the threat changes rahmandad2022_missing.

Generative artificial intelligence, large language models in particular, offered a path forward: agents whose decisions are produced by an LLM rather than by hand-coded rules, allowing behavior to emerge and adapt within the simulation rather than be fixed in advance ghaffarzadegan2024. This dissertation introduced that approach to epidemic modeling and examined its credibility along two dimensions the surrounding literature has flagged as concerns larooij_tornberg2026,liwu2025: the prompt that conditions each agent, and the large language model behind it. Across the three studies, the paradigm proved viable but conditional.

5.1 Contributions

Chapter 2 established the methodology of the dissertation by building the first generative agent-based epidemic model. One thousand LLM-powered agents were placed in a simulated town under three feedback conditions, with each agent prompted daily on whether to stay home or go out. The agents reproduced behavior at both scales of the system: at the individual level, they quarantined when symptomatic and self-isolated as community cases rose; at the population level, the model exhibited multiple infection waves followed by a low, stable endemic period. Beyond producing realistic dynamics, the chapter made a more general point: an LLM can substitute for hand-coded behavioral rules without requiring the modeler to specify those rules in advance.

Chapter 3 examined whether the prompt is a parameter of the method. Three types of perturbation were applied to the agent prompt: semantic variation, contextual shift, and persona-name substitution. Synonymous rewordings and persona-name swaps left epidemic trajectories statistically unchanged, but minor wording variations and contextual shifts moved them. The prompt is therefore a parameter along some dimensions and not others. The chapter’s contribution is twofold: it provides the field’s first prompt-sensitivity analysis for generative agents, and it maps which kinds of prompt change move a GABM’s results and which leave them intact, offering practical guidance for anyone building or interpreting one.

Chapter 4 extended the same logic from the prompt to the LLM itself. A probe across 21 LLM configurations (three providers, multiple generations, with and without reasoning) yielded 420,000 binary decisions. Most LLMs agreed on the direction of persona effects but diverged several-fold in magnitude, and the model attributes a researcher would normally consult to pick an LLM — size, version, training-data cutoff, release date — failed to predict where its behavior landed. Connecting agents to an LLM lets a modeler offload the hard problem of specifying human behavior, but the variation observed here means that move carries a hidden cost: the choice of LLM itself becomes part of the parameterization. The chapter’s contribution is the GABM field’s first cross-provider LLM-sensitivity analysis, and the finding that the LLM is not an interchangeable backend but a parameter on which every GABM result depends.

Taken together, the three chapters deliver a method and a clear account of its limits. GABM works: a large language model can stand in for hand-coded behavioral rules and reproduce realistic dynamics at both the individual and population levels. But it is not the objective, parameter-free simulation it appears to be. Every result it produces is conditional on two design choices, the prompt and the LLM, in addition to all of the parameterization a typical ABM has.

5.2 Broader Impacts

The most direct broader impact of this work is on the practice of GABM itself. As generative agents become more common in social-science simulation, the reporting standards inherited from agent-based modeling are not sufficient: a published GABM should report the prompt used, the LLM with its respective settings, and, at minimum, a small prompt and LLM sensitivity analysis.

Those standards matter because using language models to stand in for people is spreading quickly, within GABM and well beyond it. The GABM literature alone now draws critical reviews larooij_tornberg2026,liwu2025 as well as literature reviews gao2024hss,mou2024. More broadly, language models are increasingly cast as simulated humans across the social sciences, playing economic scenarios horton2023, standing in for survey samples in political polling argyle2023, and taking part in strategic games akata2023. 

For generative AI, this dissertation's contribution speaks directly to the ambition of replicating human behavior with a language model. It marks the boundary of what that replication can support: across LLMs the direction of behavioral effects is reproducible, but its magnitude is not, so qualitative conclusions drawn from generative human proxies are far more trustworthy than quantitative ones. The boundary is unsurprising given what these models are for: general-purpose LLMs are trained to be helpful, fluent assistants, not faithful samplers of the heterogeneity of a real human population. Purpose-built efforts are only beginning to target that goal directly, whether by fine-tuning on real human-action traces kolluri2025 or by designing models specifically to reproduce population-level behavioral diversity simile_ai.

For epidemic modeling, the contribution is narrower but no less real. With the technology as it stands, a generative agent-based model will not forecast an outbreak’s trajectory in numbers; quantitative forecasting still requires calibrated mechanistic models rahmandad2022_forecasting. What GABM offers instead is behavioral insight where the data needed to model behavior directly does not yet exist. It can serve as a proxy for that missing behavioral data, an initial and endogenous estimate of how a population might respond that is coupled to a conventional model. These limits, however, need not be permanent. As generative agents grow more faithful to real people, such as recent simulations built from interviews with a thousand individuals park2024_1000, the behavioral estimates they produce should grow more accurate, accurate enough to enter conventional models as a trusted behavioral input rather than a placeholder. For now, that level of fidelity remains elusive.

For industrial and systems engineering, the contribution is a method. This dissertation delivered the first generative agent-based model and, with it, a new instrument for studying sociotechnical systems, those in which human decisions and system dynamics continually shape each other. It takes its place alongside system dynamics, agent-based modeling, and discrete-event simulation, to be reached for precisely when the human-decision component of a system resists mathematical specification and must instead be drawn from an LLM — trained on the vast body of human-generated text — to emulate human behavior.

5.3 Implications for Modeling Human Behavior

The implications of this work are methodological: it does not say what to do in an epidemic, but how to think about modeling human behavior when that behavior resists clean specification. Agent-based modeling and generative agent-based modeling rest on the same numeric scaffolding of contact rates, infectivities, recovery times, and the other parameters that drive a simulated world; what separates them is the behavioral layer: how ABM and GABM each decide what an agent will do.

Classical ABM writes that behavior as an explicit equation, transparent and under the modeler’s full control but requiring the modeler to already know the rule, an impossible demand when human behavior, with all its heterogeneity, is the very thing in question. GABM instead offloads the behavioral layer to a large language model that has absorbed patterns of human decision-making from a vast corpus of human-written text, buying behavioral nuance no equation could express at the cost of opacity: the decision rule becomes a black box whose output depends on the prompt and the model. Neither approach escapes parameterization; they differ in where it lives. ABM places the modeler’s assumptions in equations one can read; GABM places them in a prompt and a model one must learn to probe.

For the modeler, the choice between ABM and GABM depends on how to build the behavioral layer: an explicit rule when agent behavior is simple enough to specify, or an LLM when it is too rich to write down. However, selecting GABM also depends on knowing when an LLM's proxy for human behavior can be trusted, a question this dissertation's sensitivity analyses begin to answer but leave open.

Earning that trust is the work that lies ahead, and it begins with validation. The behavioral realism of a generative agent-based model can only be established by comparing its output to real human behavior, such as survey responses or longitudinal records of the decisions people actually made. Two difficulties make this harder than it sounds. Because language models are trained on enormous and largely undisclosed corpora, the very data used to check them may already sit in their training, so a credible test requires held-out behavior the LLM did not ingest. Additionally, behavioral fidelity does not transfer automatically across domains, so validation has to be repeated in each new setting: an agent that captures how people act in an epidemic may misrepresent how they act as consumers in a marketing model or as drivers in a traffic simulation.

Validation can only measure behavioral fidelity; achieving it is the harder task. The goal is a generative agent whose behavior can be trusted to stand in for a real person, and progress toward it could come from three directions. The first is an LLM built or fine-tuned to reproduce human behavior, unlike LLMs today that are trained to become helpful assistants. The second is prompts that lead to higher fidelity: determining how to best represent a person in language to these LLMs. The third is sheer scale of LLMs — the bet that larger models will acquire behavioral fidelity like they unexpectedly acquired fluency in arithmetic and reasoning. 

Which of these will matter most is a question this dissertation cannot settle. What this research establishes instead is a methodology and guidance to report every GABM result with prompt and LLM sensitivity. The most exciting prospect, though, lies ahead: as generative agents draw closer to real human behavior, they offer modelers a way not only to explain how and why a population responded to the last epidemic, but to anticipate how people will respond to the next.

Appendix B · Implementation Architecture and Details

A python script is used for both the GAI and ABM part of the model. At the start of the model, agents are initialized with a name, age, traits, and starting health condition. Theirs names are selected from the names-dataset 3.1.0 Python library (https://github.com/philipperemy/name-datase), and ages are selected from 18-65 based on a probability-distribution of the US census (27). Agent traits are based on the Big Five traits typically used in psychology (20). For each of the five dimensions, there is a 50% chance of receiving a “positive” versus “negative” variant (e.g., agreeable vs. antagonistic). Depending on the model run’s initial conditions, agents will either be susceptible or infected. Each simulation proceeds in discrete time steps, where one step corresponds to a single day.

Following initialization, the GAI component begins. Each agent is connected to gpt-3.5-turbo-0301 LLM via OpenAI’s API, which functions as the reasoning engine driving daily decisions. The prompt fed into turbo GPT is a system message (also known as a developer message). System messages are used to tune the behavior of ChatGPT’s responses. System messages, compared to user messages, enhance ChatGPT’s ability to maintain character and follow rules over a prolonged conversation. Hence, when asking ChatGPT to roleplay, it was a natural fit. Secondly, to parse ChatGPT’s responses, it is important that it responds in a structured manner. We used system messages for three purposes: imbuing a persona, providing relevant context and output formatting.

The prompt sent to the LLM can be seen in Figure B.1 and includes the agent’s name, age, personality traits, and background information. Depending on the type of feedback run, the remaining context will vary. For the base run, agents are only reminded of the daily routine and fiscal motivation to work.

In the own-health feedback condition, each agent receives a health status string that reflects their current stage of infection. The disease duration is modeled as six days, with the first two days being asymptomatic but infectious. On days 3 and 6, agents are assigned the symptom “has a slight cough,” while on days 4 and 5, they experience “a cough and a fever.” Uninfected agents, as well as those in the initial asymptomatic phase or post-recovery, receive the status “feels normal.” Upon recovery, agents return to a non-infectious, recovered state.

For the societal and own-health feedback runs, agents are further informed of the town’s disease prevalence by reading a news report stating that X% of the population contracted newly detected infections the previous day. An infection counts as “detected” once an agent reaches day 4 of being infected — the first day of fever-and-cough symptoms, when a real case would typically be diagnosed. In every condition — including the base run and own-health feedback — the prompt closes by asking whether the agent wants to stay home for the day and to provide its reasoning.

The last part of the prompt is to help ChatGPT output the reasoning and response in a parsable format. If the output is uninterpretable, those non-conforming responses default to a random choice between “yes” and “no” value. Out of 68,000 data points fed into the prompt, we found that less than 0.33% of responses disobeyed the prompt orders of providing a response of either yes or no. At the end of each GAI cycle, the agent’s response is interpreted to determine whether they will stay home or go outside for that day. This decision governs whether the agent is exposed to possible contact and infection in the ABM component.

Once agents have received their prompts and generated responses, the ABM framework operationalizes these decisions to simulate daily movement and contact patterns. To simulate daily interpersonal interactions, agents who chose to go outside were assigned a contact rate based on the model’s global contact parameter (a baseline of 5 contacts) and the proportion of the population currently outside. If the effective contact rate was non-integer (e.g., 3.6), agents were assigned the integer part (3), with a probability equal to the fractional part (0.6) of receiving one additional contact. After determining each agent’s individual contact rate, the model stochastically paired agents for interaction. Each agent iteratively selected partners from the pool of other outside agents who were not already in their interaction list, continuing until their contact quota was met or the pool was exhausted. These pairings formed the basis for potential disease transmission in the subsequent infection step. Infections are directional and only occur from infected to susceptible agents. Recovered agents do not contribute to or receive infection.

Once interaction pairs are formed, the model evaluates each for potential transmission. If one agent is infected and the other is susceptible, a random number is drawn from a uniform distribution between 0 and 1. If this value falls below the model’s infectivity rate of 0.1, the susceptible agent transitions to a pre-infection state, scheduled to become infectious in the following step. This mechanism mimics the inherent randomness of real-world transmission events.

At the end of each time step, agents update their health status based on their current condition and infection timeline. Agents in the pre-infectious state (i.e., recently exposed and marked as "to be infected") transition to an infectious state and begin counting days of infection. Those already infected increment their infection day counter, and upon reaching day 7, are marked as recovered and become immune for the remainder of the simulation. Agents who are susceptible and not newly exposed retain their status. No further changes are applied to recovered individuals. Once all health statuses are updated, the model records population-level statistics, including the number of susceptible, infected, and recovered agents, the number of new infections, and total daily interactions.

Together, these parameters set the disease’s basic reproduction number. The contact parameter of 5 is a baseline maximum — realized only when the entire population is outside — while the effective contact rate scales down with the fraction of agents who go out (for example, 5 × 0.72 ≈ 3.6 contacts when 72% are outside). Using the baseline contact rate of 5, the 0.1 per-contact transmission probability, and the 6-day infectious period, the basic reproduction number is R0 = 5 × 0.1 × 6 = 3.

The simulation continues until the configured number of days is reached or until no infected agents remain for two consecutive time steps, at which point the model halts early. To support long-running experiments and recovery from interruptions, the model saves checkpoint files at the end of each day.
