Math walkthrough

A primer on what's actually being computed in the figure below, with the binary-trait case worked out, plus the link from r → r² → VIF.

1. What's in each cell of the heatmap

Not a scatter plot. Each cell is a single number: Pearson's correlation coefficient $r$, computed from the 995,000 paired observations of the two predictors that label that row and column.

Formal definition:

$$r_{XY} = \frac{\operatorname{cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_X \,\sigma_Y}$$

Where:

$$\operatorname{cov}(X, Y) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y}), \qquad \sigma_X = \sqrt{\operatorname{var}(X)}$$

Plain reading: covariance asks “when $X$ is above its mean, is $Y$ also above its mean?” If yes on average, covariance is positive. Correlation $r$ is the same thing rescaled to lie in $[-1, +1]$ by dividing out the units (the standard deviations).

2. Binary variables (phi coefficient)

The formula above doesn't change — you plug in 0s and 1s like any other numbers. But since the values are $\{0, 1\}$, every piece collapses to something simple. Let $p_X = P(X = 1)$ (the share of 1s):

$$\bar{x} = p_X, \qquad \operatorname{var}(X) = p_X(1 - p_X), \qquad \sigma_X = \sqrt{p_X(1 - p_X)}$$

And the covariance between two binary variables boils down to:

$$\operatorname{cov}(X, Y) = P(X{=}1 \text{ and } Y{=}1) - p_X \, p_Y$$

So the correlation is:

$$r_{XY} = \frac{P(X{=}1, Y{=}1) - p_X p_Y}{\sqrt{p_X(1 - p_X) \cdot p_Y(1 - p_Y)}}$$

This special case has a name: the phi coefficient ($\varphi$). It's still ordinary Pearson $r$ — just restricted to two binary variables. Same range, same interpretation.

Worked example — Extraversion ↔ Agreeableness. Both are 1/0 with independent 50/50 sampling.
  • $p_X = p_Y = 0.5$
  • Under independence: $P(X{=}1, Y{=}1) = 0.5 \times 0.5 = 0.25$
  • $\operatorname{cov} = 0.25 - 0.25 = 0$, so $r = 0$ exactly.
In our actual 995,000-row sample, empirical $P(X{=}1, Y{=}1) \approx 0.257$ (sampling noise, not 0.250 on the nose). Plugging back in:
  • $\operatorname{cov} \approx 0.257 - 0.25 = 0.007$
  • $\sigma_X \sigma_Y = 0.5 \times 0.5 = 0.25$
  • $r \approx 0.007 / 0.25 \approx 0.027$ ← matches the $+0.03$ cell in the heatmap below.
That “0.01-ish” noise floor on most cells in the heatmap is sampling jitter around an underlying truth of zero.

Binary × continuous (the “point-biserial” case)

For something like Age ↔ Extraversion — continuous mixed with binary — the formula is exactly the same Pearson formula. When one side is binary it picks up a special name (point-biserial correlation), but mathematically it's just $r$. Reading: “do extraverted agents tend to be older or younger than introverted agents, on average?” In our data that cell is $-0.006$: a tiny tilt with no practical meaning.

3. From $r$ to $r^2$ — yes, literally the square

For any two variables:

$$r^2 = (r_{XY})^2$$

Same letter, just raised to the second power. Interpretation: the fraction of variance in one variable that is linearly explained by the other.

  • If $r = 0.5$  ⇒  $r^2 = 0.25$  ⇒  “25% of $Y$’s variance is captured by a linear function of $X$.”
  • If $r = 0.027$ (Extraversion ↔ Agreeableness)  ⇒  $r^2 = 0.00073$  ⇒  0.07% shared variance. Essentially zero.
  • If $r = 0.89$ (Societal Health ↔ Societal Health²)  ⇒  $r^2 = 0.79$  ⇒  79% shared variance. Strong — but expected because a quadratic and its square are mechanically linked.

4. VIF — what we use for multicollinearity

Two-variable $r^2$ tells you how related two predictors are. But what if predictor $X_i$ is essentially a combination of several other predictors? No single pairwise $r$ would catch that. VIF generalizes the idea using multiple $R^2$:

$$\text{VIF}_i = \frac{1}{1 - R^2_{i,\text{aux}}}$$

Where $R^2_{i,\text{aux}}$ comes from fitting an auxiliary OLS regression in which predictor $X_i$ is treated as the dependent variable and every other predictor is used to explain it:

$$X_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_1 + \beta_2 X_2 + \cdots + \beta_{p-1} X_{p-1} + \varepsilon$$

$R^2_{i,\text{aux}}$ is the fraction of $X_i$’s variance that the other predictors collectively explain. Then:

  • $R^2_{\text{aux}} = 0$ (perfectly independent of all others)  ⇒  $\text{VIF} = 1$
  • $R^2_{\text{aux}} = 0.5$ (half-predictable from the others)  ⇒  $\text{VIF} = 2$
  • $R^2_{\text{aux}} = 0.9$  ⇒  $\text{VIF} = 10$  ←  typical concern threshold
  • $R^2_{\text{aux}} = 1$ (exact linear combo of the others)  ⇒  $\text{VIF} = \infty$ — the regression breaks.
Worked example — Societal Health. We measured $\text{VIF} = 4.78$. Reverse the formula: $$R^2_{\text{aux}} = 1 - \frac{1}{4.78} \approx 0.791$$ So 79.1% of Societal Health’s variance is explained by the other 10 predictors combined. Where's it coming from? Almost entirely from Societal Health² (the column right next to it). The other nine predictors barely move the needle.

5. Connecting $r^2$ and VIF

For the special case of just two predictors total, the multiple $R^2$ in VIF collapses to the squared simple $r$:

$$R^2_{\text{aux}} = r^2_{X_i X_j} \quad\implies\quad \text{VIF}_i = \frac{1}{1 - r^2_{X_i X_j}}$$

So in the trivial two-predictor case, the VIF you'd compute equals $1/(1 - r^2)$ from the pairwise correlation. When you have more than two predictors, multiple $R^2$ can grow beyond what any single pairwise $r^2$ captures, because the predictors can collectively explain $X_i$ in ways no single one does alone. That's why we report both: the heatmap shows you what's happening pairwise, and the VIF column tells you what's happening once you let all the predictors gang up.

6. What the figure tells Joe

  1. Heatmap cells are Pearson $r$, valid for any mix of binary and continuous predictors.
  2. Persona / trait / demographic cells are all near zero — confirms agents were sampled independently by construction.
  3. The single strong off-diagonal cell — Societal Health vs Societal Health² — is a known mechanical artifact, not a multicollinearity problem.
  4. VIFs ≈ 1 everywhere else, so no hidden multi-predictor collinearity either.

In other words: the figure is the cleanest possible covariance result. Joe was right to ask — we just didn't have the receipts. Now we do.
